Sector 4 — Up-type Quarks

CP² geometry, three colours from a single Euler characteristic, and the origin of the strong force.

Geometry

The d=4 sector has geometry CP² — the complex projective plane — with isometry group SU(3). CP² is a Kähler manifold: it has a complex structure and a natural γ₅ operator from that structure, which provides the chirality of up-type quarks. This is why up quarks have left-handed weak coupling — not by assumption, but because the Kähler geometry of CP² selects a chirality.

CP² is also the colour sector. Its Euler characteristic \(\chi(\mathbb{CP}^2) = 3\) directly gives the number of quark colours: \(N_c = 3\). The three independent modes of the d=4 sector geometry are exactly the three colour states of a quark. They transform under SU(3) — the isometry group of CP² — in the fundamental representation, which is why colour is an SU(3) symmetry. None of this is postulated; it follows from the geometry of the manifold.

The charm and top quarks receive an additional Generation Tower Correction (GTC) from the ℓ=2 component of the sector kernel:

\[ \varepsilon = \frac{1}{280\sqrt{7}} \approx 0.001350 \]

This correction applies at depths \(\{0, n_u, S(n_u,3)\} = \{0, 3, 10\}\) — the up quark baseline, and then charm and top. The GTC closes the charm/up ratio error to 0.000% and the top/up error to −0.048%. The top quark mode index \(n = 72 = N_c(N_c+1)(N_c+3)\) is derived from the Euler characteristic \(N_c = 3\) alone.

The geometric origin of colour

In the Standard Model, \(N_c = 3\) is an experimental input. In IDWT it is a theorem: \(N_c = \chi(\mathbb{CP}^2) = 3\). The Euler characteristic of \(\mathbb{CP}^2\) is 3 because \(\mathbb{CP}^2\) has one cell in each of the dimensions 0, 2, and 4 — three cells total, alternating in sign — giving \(\chi = 1 - 0 + 1 - 0 + 1 = 3\).

Those three independent modes of the \(\mathbb{CP}^2\) geometry are the three colour states per quark. They are not three copies of something; they are the three geometrically independent directions of the sector space, and they transform into each other under the SU(3) isometry of \(\mathbb{CP}^2\). Colour is not a property attached to quarks from outside — it is what it means to be a mode in the \(d=4\) sector.

SU(3) colour symmetry follows from the requirement that physics not depend on the local orientation of the colour frame in the \(d=4\) sector. Different orientations of the three colour directions are physically equivalent — this requirement forces local SU(3) invariance, which is what we call colour gauge symmetry. It is a consistency requirement of the geometry, not a postulate.

Because \(N_c = \chi(\mathbb{CP}^2) = 3 = n_u\), and \(n_s = n_u + 1 = 4 = \chi(\mathbb{CP}^3)\), all sector coupling constants are functions of \(N_c\) alone.

Coupling constant

The d=4 sector self-coupling is \(g_{44} = 12/\sqrt{7}\), derived from \(n_s\) and \(n_u\):

\[ g_{44} = \frac{n_s \cdot n_u}{\sqrt{n_s + n_u}} = \frac{12}{\sqrt{7}} \approx 4.536 \]

T9a: \(g_{33} \times g_{44} = 8\sqrt{7} \times 12/\sqrt{7} = 96\) exactly. This product is the Hopf universality product for sectors \(d=3\) and \(d=4\), equal to \(N_c(N_c+1)^3/2 = 96\).
