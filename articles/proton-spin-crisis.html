The Proton Spin Crisis and Sector Orbital Angular Momentum

In 1987, the EMC collaboration measured the spin carried by quarks inside the proton and found roughly 30% — far less than expected. Three decades of experiment have narrowed the gap but have not closed it. In IDWT the proton is a d=3/d=4 composite whose modes carry sector orbital angular momentum in the hidden directions, providing a structural account of where the rest of the spin resides.

The crisis

The proton has spin \(\tfrac{1}{2}\). For forty years after the quark model was established, it was assumed that this spin came almost entirely from the intrinsic spins of the three valence quarks: two up quarks and one down quark, combining to give \(\tfrac{1}{2}\). The sea quarks and gluons were thought to contribute very little.

The European Muon Collaboration measured the quark spin contribution at CERN by scattering polarized muons off polarized protons and mapping out the spin structure function \(g_1(x)\). Their 1987 result, confirmed and refined many times since, is that quark spins carry only about 25–30% of the proton's total spin. Integrating over the full Bjorken-\(x\) range:

\[ \Delta\Sigma = \Delta u + \Delta d + \Delta s \approx 0.30 \]

where \(\Delta q\) is the contribution of each quark flavor's intrinsic spin to the proton spin. This is the proton spin puzzle, sometimes called the spin crisis.

The angular momentum sum rule says the total spin must be accounted for:

\[ \tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{1}{2}\Delta\Sigma + L_q + \Delta G + L_G \]

where \(L_q\) is the orbital angular momentum of quarks, \(\Delta G\) is the gluon helicity, and \(L_G\) is the gluon orbital angular momentum. Three decades of polarized DIS, RHIC spin measurements, and lattice QCD have established that gluon helicity \(\Delta G\) is positive but not large enough by itself to close the gap. The quark orbital contribution \(L_q\) is experimentally difficult to access — it requires generalized parton distributions (GPDs), not forward parton distributions — and remains poorly constrained. The puzzle is not fully resolved.

The proton in IDWT

In IDWT the proton is a colour-singlet composite of quark modes in the d=3 and d=4 sectors (Part 8 of the notes). The d=3 sector (\(S^3\)) hosts the down-type quarks; the d=4 sector (\(CP^2\)) hosts the up-type quarks. The three valence quarks of the proton (uud) reside in the joint d=3/d=4 sector space, bound into a colour-singlet configuration by the kernel coupling \(g_{3,4}\).

The proton's mode in sector space is not a pure ground state. The cross-sector kernel term \((\xi_3 \cdot \xi_4)^2\) — the coupling between the d=3 and d=4 sector coordinates — induces a small admixture of \(l=1\) sector angular momentum into what is otherwise an \(l=0\) ground state. That is: the proton's wave function in the combined \(S^3 \times CP^2\) sector space has a dominant \(l=0\) component and a small \(l=1\) component in the relative sector coordinate. The \(l=1\) component carries orbital angular momentum in the hidden sector directions (Part 8 §10 of the notes).

This is not a free parameter introduced to fix the spin budget. The same \(l=1\) admixture that produces the sector orbital angular momentum is the mechanism for the observed nucleon magnetic moments. The IDWT predictions for the proton and neutron magnetic moments are

\[ \mu_p \approx 2.793\,\mu_N \quad ({\rm PDG:}\; 2.7928,\; +0.01\%) \] \[ \mu_n \approx -1.913\,\mu_N \quad ({\rm PDG:}\; -1.9130,\; +0.02\%) \]

both following from the colour-singlet projector structure of the uud and udd configurations with the same \(l=1\) sector admixture. The sector orbital angular momentum is not invented to explain the spin crisis — it is already present in the mechanism that reproduces the magnetic moments.

Where the missing spin resides

The spin crisis asks why quark intrinsic spins carry only \(\approx 30\%\) of the proton spin. The sum rule in IDWT has the same form as QCD, but the sector orbital contribution \(L_q\) has a specific structural identity: it is the orbital angular momentum of the quark modes in the d=3 and d=4 sector directions, induced by the \((\xi_3 \cdot \xi_4)^2\) cross-sector kernel term.

In the standard QCD account, orbital angular momentum is angular momentum of quarks in three-dimensional space — quarks orbiting around each other inside the proton. In IDWT, there is that contribution, but there is also angular momentum in the hidden sector directions: the \(l=1\) admixture in the sector-space wave function carries angular momentum in coordinates that a d=3 observer cannot directly resolve. When the proton's spin is measured via deep inelastic scattering, the probe couples through the electromagnetic vertex on the observable d=3 coordinates. It measures quark spins and quark orbital angular momentum in the observable directions. It does not couple to the sector orbital angular momentum in the hidden d=3 and d=4 coordinate directions beyond the observable three.

This is the IDWT-specific content: the spin crisis is a consequence of the proton being a d=3/d=4 composite in a sector space with more coordinates than a d=3 observer can probe. The missing spin is not missing — it is angular momentum in the additional d=3 and d=4 sector dimensions, present in the mode structure but invisible to the electromagnetic probe.

Concretely: the proton lives in the full \(S^3 \times CP^2\) sector space, which has \(3 + 4 = 7\) total dimensions. A polarized DIS probe couples to the quark fields via a photon vertex on the three observable coordinates. The sector-orbital contribution from the additional four coordinates — the three \(CP^2\) directions and the relative \(S^3\) phase — is not picked up. The quark spin sum rule measures \(\Delta\Sigma\); it measures the spin in the observable directions; it misses the sector orbital piece.

The axial coupling prediction

A related observable that is cleanly predicted in IDWT is the nucleon axial coupling \(g_A\), which determines the rate of neutron beta decay and the coupling of the axial current to the nucleon. IDWT gives

\[ g_A = \sqrt{\frac{S(n_s+1,\,3)}{S(n_s,\,3)}} = \sqrt{\frac{35}{20}} = \sqrt{\frac{7}{4}} = 1.3229 \]

where \(n_s = 4\) is the composite mode number (\(n_s = n_d + n_u = 1+3\)) and \(S(n,d) = \binom{n+d-1}{d}\) is the hockey-stick mode count. The PDG value is \(g_A = 1.2723 \pm 0.0023\), a discrepancy of \(+4.0\%\). The formula gives the ratio of successive d=3 mode counts at the seed level — a purely combinatorial quantity from the sector structure — with no free parameters tuned to the measurement.

The \(g_A\) discrepancy is likely connected to the same sector orbital admixture that affects the spin budget. The axial coupling measured in neutron decay is sensitive to the same \(\Delta\Sigma\) and to the same \(l=1\) orbital mixing that sets the magnetic moments. A more complete derivation of \(g_A\) from the kernel matrix elements — rather than from the mode-count ratio — would provide the link. That derivation is an open item (Part 8 §10 of the notes).

Status and what would be needed

The structural picture is in place (🔶): the proton is a d=3/d=4 composite, the \(l=1\) sector admixture exists and reproduces the magnetic moments, and the hidden-direction orbital angular momentum provides the structural home for the missing spin fraction. What is not yet in place is a closed derivation of the effective coupling \(g_{3,4}^{\rm eff}\) at the baryon scale from the kernel matrix elements. Part 8 §10 estimates this coupling as \(g_{3,4}^{\rm eff} \approx 125\), significantly larger than the kernel-level value \(g_{3,4} = 4\sqrt{6} \approx 9.80\), and the enhancement factor of \(\approx 12.8\times\) has not been derived from first principles. With two parameters (\(g_{3,4}^{\rm eff}\) and the overlap factor \(f_{\rm overlap} = 0.72\)) fitting two observables (proton and neutron magnetic moments), this is a consistency check rather than a parameter-free prediction.

To turn the spin crisis identification into a quantitative test, two things are needed. First, the kernel-level derivation of \(g_{3,4}^{\rm eff}\): computing the \(l=1\) admixture amplitude directly from the \((\xi_3 \cdot \xi_4)^2\) matrix elements in the sector Hamiltonian, rather than estimating it phenomenologically. Second, an IDWT prediction for the quark helicity fraction \(\Delta\Sigma\) itself: if the fraction of the proton spin carried by quark intrinsic spins is determined by the sector geometry (by how much of the sector wave function is in the \(l=0\) vs \(l=1\) component), then it is predicted, not fitted. That prediction could then be compared to the measured \(\Delta\Sigma \approx 0.30\).
