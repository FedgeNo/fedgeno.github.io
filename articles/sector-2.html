Sector 2 — Gauge Bosons

The simplest non-trivial sector. Two directions, U(1) geometry, four particles — and the coordinates that every other particle carries inside it.

Geometry

The d=2 sector has geometry CP¹ — the complex projective line, topologically a two-sphere. Its isometry group is U(1). This is the smallest sector in the hidden space: two spatial degrees of freedom, the minimum needed for a non-trivial coupling structure.

CP¹ is also the first space in the complex Hopf fibration chain — S¹ → S³ → CP¹ — from which the entire sector set is built. Every higher sector in IDWT contains the d=2 directions as a coordinate subspace. The d=2 sector is in this sense the foundation of the whole tower.

Particles

Four particles live in the d=2 sector: the photon, the W± bosons, the Z⁰ boson, and the Higgs.

The photon has mode index n=0. S(0,d) = 0 for any d, making the photon exactly massless — not by assumption but because zero quanta distributed across any number of directions gives zero mass. The W, Z, and Higgs are excitations of the same d=2 sector at higher mode indices, distinguished by their winding in the U(1) Hopf fiber.

Coupling filter

The U(1) geometry of CP¹ gives the photon its two polarization states — helicity +1 and helicity −1. These are not an arbitrary choice; they are the complete set of independent directions in a 2D space. A photon couples to whatever is aligned with its polarization and is blocked by everything perpendicular. This is the orientation filter of electromagnetism.

The W and Z bosons also live in d=2 and couple through the same sector, but via the SU(2)L × SU(2)R structure that the d=3 sector's SO(4) geometry introduces. The weak coupling is chiral — it only reaches left-handed components of fermions — because the Kähler chirality of the d=2 sector selects one handedness when paired with the d=3 sector above it.

The universal sector

The d=2 sector is the reference for the Stage-1 observability criterion. A mode (n,d) is observable in our d=3 world only if its dimensional depth Ωlog = ln(S(n,d)/S(n,2)) ≤ ln 2. This measures how much of the mode's activity is concentrated in the d=2 directions relative to the full d-dimensional activity. The d=2 sector is the anchor because it is the intersection of all sector wavefunctions: every particle above d=2 contains the d=2 coordinates exactly, and the observability criterion checks whether enough activity reaches those shared directions to be measurable.

Because d=2 is nested inside every higher sector, every particle with d ≥ 2 has the photon's coordinates in its wavefunction. This is the geometric origin of the universality of electric charge: coupling to electromagnetism does not require a special property — it requires only that you contain the d=2 directions, which every particle does.

The one exception is the photon itself, which has d=2 exactly and therefore has no hidden sector activity beyond those two directions. It appears in our 3D world as a clean transverse wave because there is nothing more to it than the two transverse directions it already occupies.

Coupling constant

The d=2 sector self-coupling is g22 = 722.5, derived from ns = 4 alone:

g22 = (S(ns,3) − nu)² (S(nu,4) − S(nu,3)) / 2 = 722.5

This large value reflects the d=2 sector's role as the universal reference. The PMNS mixing angles are determined by g55 = 96/g22, connecting neutrino mixing directly to the gauge boson coupling scale. The fine structure constant α and the electroweak mixing angle sin²θW are both derived from the d=2 mode indices and g22.

The coupling scale of this sector — where IDWT couplings are defined before QED/QCD running — is the d=2 sector scale, numerically close to mW.
