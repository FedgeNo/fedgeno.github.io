# Shared Dimensions and Perception in Infinite Dimensional Wave Theory

In IDWT, dimensions are **accumulated** as sector dimensionality increases. Lower-dimensional sectors form the foundational layers upon which higher sectors are built.

- The **d=2 sector** (CP¹ geometry) provides the **base 2 dimensions**. These are universally shared and form the common geometric foundation for the entire theory.
- The **d=3 sector** adds **one additional dimension**, bringing the total to **three dimensions** that we perceive as ordinary space.
- Higher sectors accumulate further dimensions on top of this base.

Our perception is limited to these **three accumulated dimensions** (the two foundational from d=2 plus the one added by d=3). All dimensions beyond these three are sector-specific and lie outside direct perception.

## The Electron (d=6 Sector)

- The electron is a **6-dimensional spherical standing wave** residing in the d=6 sector.
- This resonance exists coherently across the **2 foundational dimensions**, the **1 additional accumulated dimension** (making the perceivable 3D), and **3 further sector-specific dimensions**.
- What we call an **orbital** is **our perception** of the electron’s **6D orbit** around an atomic nucleus, restricted to the **three accumulated dimensions** we can perceive.

## Massive Bosons in the d=2 Sector

All electroweak gauge bosons and the Higgs reside in the same d=2 sector. The photon, as the massless n=0 mode, possesses only the two transverse polarization states natural to the 2D geometry.  

When mass is acquired (at higher mode indices n=76 for W± and n=81 for Z), the bosons gain a **third, longitudinal degree of freedom**. This extra polarization emerges because the mass term, generated by the sector potential, breaks the gauge symmetry and allows the previously unphysical longitudinal mode to become a real, propagating degree of freedom. The Higgs (n=95) appears as a scalar (spin-0) resonance in the same sector — the radial excitation without vector structure.

This mechanism unifies the entire electroweak sector within the minimal d=2 geometry: mass literally unlocks an additional hidden direction within the shared foundational space.

## Core Principle

Dimensions in IDWT are built cumulatively: d=2 supplies the universal base of 2 dimensions, d=3 adds the third dimension that completes our perceivable space, and higher sectors add further hidden dimensions on top of that foundation.

- Photons appear classical because they reside entirely within the foundational shared dimensions.
- Massive bosons (W, Z) acquire their longitudinal polarization through the mass term, which unlocks an additional degree of freedom within the same d=2 geometry.
- Electrons (and particles in higher sectors) appear quantum and orbital-like because a significant portion of their 6D orbit exists in additional dimensions beyond the three we perceive.

The orbital is therefore **our perception of the electron’s true 6D orbit**, filtered through the three accumulated spatial dimensions made available by the d=2 and d=3 sectors.

## How Accumulated Dimensionality Affects Particles in IDWT

In Infinite Dimensional Wave Theory, dimensions are **accumulated** as sector dimensionality increases. The d=2 sector provides the base 2 dimensions, the d=3 sector adds the third perceivable dimension, and higher sectors add further dimensions on top of this foundation. Our perception is limited to these three accumulated dimensions.

The number of **hidden dimensions** (beyond the perceivable 3) primarily determines how much of a particle’s full behavior lies outside direct perception. Mass is set separately by the sector scale \( m_{\text{scale},d} \) and mode index \( S(n,d) \).

### d=3 Sector (Down-type Quarks: d, s, b)

- **Total dimensions**: 3  
- **Hidden dimensions**: 0  

**Effects**:  
These quarks reside entirely within the three perceivable dimensions. Their resonances have no hidden components, leading to strong, direct interactions and tight color confinement within hadrons.

### d=4 Sector (Up-type Quarks: u, c, t)

- **Total dimensions**: 4  
- **Hidden dimensions**: 1  

**Effects**:  
The single extra hidden dimension introduces additional structure that manifests in flavor physics, CKM mixing, and generation-dependent mass corrections, while most of the wavefunction remains perceivable.

### d=5 Sector (Neutrinos)

- **Total dimensions**: 5  
- **Hidden dimensions**: 2  

**Effects**:  
The two hidden dimensions make neutrinos couple very weakly to matter. Their extremely small sector scale \( m_{\text{scale},5} \) (set by Hopf consistency conditions) keeps them extremely light despite the hidden dimensions.

### d=6 Sector (Electron and Muon)

- **Total dimensions**: 6  
- **Hidden dimensions**: 3  

**Effects**:  
The electron is a 6-dimensional spherical standing wave following a genuine **6D orbit** in the full d=6 sector.  
The three hidden dimensions mean that a large part of this 6D orbit lies outside our direct perception.  
What we call an **orbital** is our restricted perception of that full 6D orbit, visible only through the three accumulated dimensions.  
This explains the rich nodal structure and “fuzzy” appearance of atomic orbitals — they are 3D slices of actual 6D orbits.

### d=10 Sector (Tau Lepton)

- **Total dimensions**: 10  
- **Hidden dimensions**: 7  

**Effects**:  
The tau has seven hidden dimensions, so most of its 10D orbit lies far outside our perception. This large hidden component, combined with its higher mode index, makes the tau significantly heavier and short-lived compared to the electron and muon.

**Core Principle**:  
Hidden dimensions do not directly cause mass. They determine how much of a particle’s full higher-dimensional orbit is inaccessible to our perception. Particles in higher-d sectors therefore appear more “quantum” because we only see a partial view of their true multi-dimensional orbits.

The orbital is **not** internal structure — it is our limited 3D perception of the electron’s actual 6D orbit.
