How the sector manifolds share space

In IDWT the six sector manifolds do not occupy separate background spaces. Each Ξ_d is built from the same underlying set of independent spatial directions. What changes from one sector to the next is how many of those directions are active.

The d=2 sector, CP¹, has two spatial degrees of freedom. This is the photon sector, and the restriction to two independent directions is why light has only two transverse polarizations.

Every higher sector contains those same two directions plus additional ones orthogonal to them. The d=3 sector, S³, adds one direction for a total of three — this is the down-quark sector. That third direction is present identically in d=4, d=5, d=6, and d=10. The fourth direction, introduced at d=4, carries through into d=5, d=6, and d=10. Each new direction, once introduced, persists in every sector above it.

d=2, CP¹, 2 degrees of freedom, Photon, W, Z, H
d=3, S³, 2+1 degrees of freedom, Down-type quarks
d=4, CP², 2+2 degrees of freedom, Up-type quarks
d=5, S⁵, 2+3 degrees of freedom, Neutrinos
d=6, CP³, 2+4 degrees of freedom, Electron, muon
d=10, CP⁵, 2+8 degrees of freedom, Tau

A sector is not a location. At any point in the shared base, all six sector potentials exist simultaneously. A photon is an excitation that uses the two base directions. A down quark uses those two plus one more. An up quark uses those two plus two more. The distinction between particle types is not where an excitation sits but how many independent spatial directions its mode occupies.

The nesting — each sector containing all the directions of every sector below it — is what governs coupling. A force confined to d=4 can only reach particles with wavefunction support in those four directions. Gravity has no such confinement and spans all ten, which is why it couples to everything and why G_N = G_fund / V_7 rather than G_fund.
