The Two-Stage Filter

Every (n, d) pair is a resonance of Ψ∞. Two successive geometric conditions select the fifteen that are observable. The rest exist — they just don't pass.

All modes exist

The wave field Ψ∞ on the infinite-dimensional manifold M∞ admits resonant modes at every pair (n, d) where d is one of the six sector dimensions {2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10} and n is any positive integer. Every such pair is a genuine eigenmode — it has a frequency, a wavefunction, and a mass given by S(n,d) × mscale,d. The full spectrum is a vast lattice of modes, essentially infinite.

Yet we observe exactly fifteen particles. The question is not which modes are allowed — all are — but which are observable from our position inside M∞ at the d=3 coordinate level. Two successive geometric filters answer this.

Stage 1: Dimensional visibility

We are inside M∞ at the d=3 coordinate level. We do not resolve all of a mode's coordinates — we resolve only its activity in the d=3 coordinates that constitute our space. A mode whose wavefunction is mostly concentrated in the d>3 sector coordinates has very little of its activity in our coordinates. The d=3-visible fraction of mode (n,d) is proportional to S(n,2)/S(n,d).

The dimensional depth of a mode is:

Ωlog(n, d) = ln(S(n, d) / S(n, 2))

Ωlog measures how much of the mode's vibrational activity lies in d>3 sector coordinates relative to the d=2 gauge baseline. A mode with Ωlog = 0 lives entirely in d=2 directions — fully visible. A mode with large Ωlog has most of its activity in the d>3 sector coordinates.

Stage 1 requires Ωlog(n, d) ≤ ln 2, which means S(n,d)/S(n,2) ≤ 2. At most half the mode's vibrational activity may be hidden from us. This is the dimensional visibility threshold: modes that concentrate more than half their activity in d>3 sector coordinates are observationally obscured and do not appear as stable particles.

Quarks (d=3, d=4) pass Stage 1 because colour confinement forces their activity into d≤4 — a gauge-forbidden operator prevents any mode leakage beyond d=4. Charged leptons and neutrinos pass at their specific mode indices because at those n values, the ratio S(n,d)/S(n,2) happens to stay within the threshold. Higher mode indices in the same sectors fail Stage 1.

Stage 2: Co-fixed-point condition

Stage 1 narrows the visible modes down considerably, but not to fifteen. Stage 2 applies a purely algebraic condition on the mode index n.

A mode index n is a co-fixed-point of the sector comb filtration generated by ns = 4 if and only if the hockey-stick recursion S(n+1, d) = S(n, d) + S(n, d−1) closes at n — meaning n participates in one of the generation laws or is a simplex image of the seed. Modes for which the recursion does not close are unstable resonances of M∞: they exist as eigenvalues of the Dirac operator, but they are not stable under the sector coupling dynamics and decay (or are never produced) before becoming observable.

This is a purely algebraic condition. It has nothing to say about QCD, colour, or sector-specific dynamics — it applies uniformly across every sector from the same starting seed ns = 4. The same filtration that selects the electron mode in d=6 also selects the strange quark mode in d=3 and the second neutrino mode in d=5. Cross-sector uniformity is a consequence of the coordinate nesting: all sectors share the same underlying M∞, so the same algebraic filter applies throughout.

There are no additional stable particles waiting to be discovered — not because the spectrum is truncated by an assumption, but because no further (n,d) pair satisfies both geometric conditions simultaneously. Any new particle would require either a new sector (excluded by the sector set theorem T3) or a new co-fixed-point (exhaustively excluded).

What the unobservable modes are

The vast majority of (n, d) pairs fail one or both filters. This does not mean they are absent from nature. Every mode that fails Stage 1 exists as a resonance of Ψ∞ — it simply has too little d=3 activity to register as a particle to a d=3 observer. Every mode that fails Stage 2 exists as a short-lived or non-normalizable state of the sector dynamics.

The unobservable modes include: all modes at large n in d=6 and d=10 (Stage 1 failure — too much activity in extra directions); all mode indices between the observed ones that fail to be simplex images of the seeds (Stage 2 failure); and all modes in sectors d≥11, which cannot be stable at all (no mode can localise in d≥11, by the Aubry-André subcriticality theorem T5).

What we call "the particle spectrum" is the d=3-coordinate readout of a far larger mathematical structure. What we resolve is determined by where we are — inside M∞ at d=3 — and by which integer the sector coupling dynamics has settled on as its seed. Two facts about geometry. Fifteen particles. Everything else follows.
