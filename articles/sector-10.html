Sector 10 — The Tau

The terminal sector. The Aubry-André critical point. One particle, fractal coupling, and the most precise mass prediction in the theory.

Geometry

The d=10 sector has geometry CP⁵ — the complex projective 5-space — with isometry group SU(6)/U(5). CP⁵ has ten real dimensions, making d=10 the terminal sector of the hidden space. The jump from d=6 to d=10 adds four new hidden directions at once; the gap d = 7, 8, 9 is not skipped arbitrarily — those dimensions have no valid sector geometry in IDWT and are genuinely absent from the hidden space.

d=10 is the terminal sector by necessity. The Sector Set Theorem (T3) shows that d=10 is the unique value at which the Gegenbauer polynomial coupling coefficient bk₀(d) equals exactly 1/2 — the Aubry-André criticality threshold. At d < 10, modes are supercritical (extended states). At d = 10, modes are critical (Cantor-set spectrum). At d > 10, modes are subcritical and cannot localise at all. The sequence terminates because d ≥ 11 cannot support stable particle modes.

Because the sectors are nested, the d=10 sector contains all ten hidden dimensions and all lower sector directions inside it. This is also the full hidden space Ξ10 — the tau's sector is not a small corner of the hidden space but the whole thing.

The tau mass prediction is the most precise in the theory: −0.001%, or −0.14σ from the PDG central value. This precision requires the Dyson resummation correction — a consequence of sitting at the Aubry-André critical point:

δτ = 1 / (nu · ns² · S(ns,4)) = 1/1680

mτ = S(23,10) × mscale,10 × (1 + 1/1680) = 1776.84 MeV

The denominator 1680 = 3 × 16 × 35 = nu × ns² × S(ns,4) follows entirely from the seed integer and the d=4 simplex number. No mass data enters the correction — it is determined by the algebraic structure of the theory.

The need for a Dyson resummation (all-orders summation rather than a perturbative correction) is the mathematical signature of the Aubry-André critical point. At criticality, the naive eigenvalue calculation does not converge perturbatively — the correction requires resumming the full perturbation series. The tau's mass sits at the edge of localisation, and that edge forces the resummation.

The Aubry-André critical point

The Aubry-André transition is a metal-insulator transition in condensed matter physics, occurring when a quasiperiodic potential reaches a critical strength. At criticality the spectrum is a Cantor set: measure zero, but topologically dense. IDWT identifies d=10 as this critical point in the hidden sector tower.

A Cantor-set spectrum for the tau means:

  • Dense: every energy neighbourhood contains a resonance. The tau can always find a decay channel — there is no energy gap protecting it.
  • Measure zero: the probability weight on any specific channel is suppressed. No single decay mode dominates.

These two properties together explain the tau's character: it is heavy (a large mode index n=23 is required to localise at the critical point), short-lived (the dense spectrum means decay channels are always available), but has a broad and distributed decay pattern (the measure-zero structure spreads coupling weight across many channels).

The Aubry-André condition bk₀(d) = 1/2 is equivalent to 4k₀ = (d−2)², giving d = 2(ns+1) = 10. This connects the terminal sector to the seed integer: d=10 is forced by ns=4.

Coupling filter

The tau's coupling filter is qualitatively different from every other sector. Lower sectors have coupling filters that block specific classes of interaction: the photon blocks misaligned currents, down quarks lack right-handed weak coupling, neutrinos forbid Majorana interactions, electrons have total colour silence. Each is a definite exclusion.

The tau's filter is not a block — it is fractal marginal availability. Because the tau's coordinates span all ten dimensions (all lower sectors are contained within d=10), it can in principle couple to everything. But the Cantor-set spectrum means that coupling weight is distributed across a fractal structure: universally available but measure-zero at every specific channel. The tau is not blocked from anything, and not strongly coupled to anything. It is everywhere and nowhere simultaneously.

The coupling constant g10,10 = 1/ns = 1/4 = g66 — the same as the d=6 charged lepton sector. This μ-τ coupling symmetry is why the electron and muon (d=6) and the tau (d=10) share a mass scale despite the large mass difference: g66 = g10,10 forces the same sector mass scale, and the mass difference comes entirely from the difference in mode indices (n=13 and n=35 for e and μ vs n=23 for τ in the context of the d=10 simplex counting).

Why d=10 terminates the sequence

Sectors d=7, 8, 9 are absent. This is not an approximation or a gap to be filled — d=7, 8, 9 have no valid sector geometry. The coupling formula for hypothetical d=7, 8, 9 sectors has no fixed point, and the coordinate nesting Ξ6 ⊂ Ξ10 jumps directly from d=6 to d=10 with nothing in between.

Sectors d ≥ 11 are not absent in the same way — they have geometry, but modes in those sectors cannot localise. The Aubry-André criticality analysis shows that for d > 10 the seed resonance enters the subcritical regime: the coupling coefficient bk₀(d) < 1/2, which means the wavefunction cannot form a normalisable bound state. Extended scattering states exist, but no stable particle modes. This is why the d > 10 part of M∞ is empty: not because those dimensions don't exist, but because the geometry cannot support localised particles there.
