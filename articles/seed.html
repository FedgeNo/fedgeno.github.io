Why \(n_s\) = 4

The single non-trivial integer input to IDWT is not a free parameter. It is the unique positive integer satisfying a cross-sector fixed-point equation — and it equals the Euler characteristic of the lepton sector manifold.

Two inputs, one of which is an integer

IDWT derives all fifteen particle masses from two inputs: the measured electron mass \(m_e = 0.511\) MeV, which sets the energy scale, and the integer \(n_s = 4\), which determines the structure of the entire spectrum. The electron mass is a unit of energy, inevitable as a scale anchor. The integer \(n_s\) is not a choice — it is forced.

The two seeds of the spectrum are \(n_{\text{down}} = 1\) (the universal ground state, which requires no argument) and \(n_{\text{strange}} = 4 = n_s\) (the strange quark's mode index, which requires one). From these two integers — and nothing else — every mode index in the spectrum follows by the hockey-stick identity. \(n_s = 4\) is the only integer for which all fifteen resulting masses match the Standard Model spectrum. The question is why.

The strange quark pins it

The strange quark occupies mode n = 4 in the d=3 sector. The muon occupies mode n = 35 in the d=6 sector. These two assignments are connected by a single equation:

\[ S(n_s,\,4) = n_{\text{muon}} \]

In words: the simplex number of the strange quark's mode index evaluated at sector dimension 4 must equal the muon's mode index. This is the cross-sector fixed-point condition. It has exactly one positive integer solution: \(n_s = 4\), because \(S(4,4) = \binom{7}{4} = 35 = n_{\text{muon}}\). No other positive integer \(n\) satisfies \(S(n,4) = 35\):

  • \(S(1,4) = 1\)
  • \(S(2,4) = 5\)
  • \(S(3,4) = 15\)
  • \(S(4,4) = 35\) ✓
  • \(S(5,4) = 70\)

The muon's mode index is what it is — \(35 = S(4,4)\) — because the hockey-stick identity forces \(n_{\text{muon}} = n_{\text{charm}} + n_{\nu_2} = S(4,3) + S(3,4) = 20 + 15 = 35\), a consequence of the Pascal recursion at \((n=4,\,d=4)\). And \(n_{\text{strange}} = 4\) is the unique integer whose \(d=4\) image lands on this number. The strange quark's mode index is pinned by the muon's mode, and both are pinned by \(n_s\).

The seed uniqueness theorem (T4)

A deeper characterisation comes from comparing two cross-sector coupling ratios simultaneously. Define:

\[ r(n) = \frac{n(n+1)}{S(n,4)} \qquad r'(n) = \frac{(n-1)n}{S(n-1,5)} \]

These are the normalized cross-sector coupling coefficients for mode \(n\) in the \(d=4\) and \(d=5\) sectors respectively. Theorem T4 states: there is a unique positive integer \(n_s\) for which \(r(n_s) = r'(n_s)\). That integer is \(n_s = 4\), with \(n_u = n_s - 1 = 3\).

Verification:

\[ r(4) = \frac{4 \times 5}{S(4,4)} = \frac{20}{35} = \frac{4}{7} \qquad r'(4) = \frac{3 \times 4}{S(3,5)} = \frac{12}{21} = \frac{4}{7} \checkmark \]

Both ratios equal \(4/7\) if and only if \(n_s = 4\). This is not a numerical coincidence — it is why the coupling coefficient can be written as the universal \(g_{\text{coeff}} = 2/\sqrt{7}\) for both the \(d=3\) and \(d=4\) sectors simultaneously. The seed \(n_s = 4\) is the unique value at which the coupling algebra of the two quark sectors locks into a self-consistent fixed point. Any other integer produces different values of \(r\) and \(r'\), breaking the cross-sector consistency.

The equal ratio \(4/7\) is itself forced: \(S(4,4) = 35\) and \(n_s(n_s+1) = 20\) give \(20/35 = 4/7\). The 4 in the numerator is \(n_s\); the \(7 = n_s + n_u\) is the sum of both seeds. This sum appears throughout the coupling structure — it is what makes \(g_{33} = 8\sqrt{7}\) and \(g_{44} = 12/\sqrt{7}\) and \(g_{33} \times g_{44} = 96\) exact identities.

The Euler characteristic connection

The integer \(n_s = 4\) has a geometric identity: it is the Euler characteristic \(\chi(\mathbb{CP}^3)\) of the \(d=6\) lepton sector manifold \(\mathbb{CP}^3\). This is Theorem T15.

\[ n_s = 4 = \chi(\mathbb{CP}^3) \quad \text{(lepton sector — } d=6 \text{)} \] \[ n_u = 3 = \chi(\mathbb{CP}^2) = N_c \quad \text{(up-quark sector — } d=4 \text{)} \]

\(N_c = 3\) is the number of quark colours. \(\chi(\mathbb{CP}^2) = 3 = N_c\) is independently derived in IDWT as the source of the SU(3) colour symmetry (see Colour from Topology). The connection \(n_u = N_c = \chi(\mathbb{CP}^2)\) and \(n_s = n_u + 1 = \chi(\mathbb{CP}^3)\) means: the seed integer of the mass spectrum is the Euler characteristic of the lepton manifold, and the quark-sector integer is the Euler characteristic of the colour manifold. Both are topological invariants of the sector geometries. The mass spectrum is organised by topology.

An Euler characteristic cannot be 3.7 or 4.2. It is an integer. That is why \(n_s\) must be an integer and why a non-integer choice has no geometric meaning. The spectrum of particle masses is quantized in units set by discrete topological invariants, not by a continuous parameter that happens to be tuned to an integer.

What follows from \(n_s = 4\)

Once \(n_s = 4\) is identified, the following all follow by calculation with no further input (beyond \(m_e\) for dimensional scale):

  • All six sector self-coupling constants \(g_{dd}\) (Theorem T9)
  • All fifteen mode indices, via the hockey-stick generation laws
  • All fifteen particle masses, via \(m(n,d) = S(n,d) \times m_{\text{scale},d}\)
  • All three PMNS neutrino mixing angles
  • The CP-violating phase \(\delta_{CP} = \pi + 2\theta_{13}\)
  • Newton's constant (up to the remaining open spectral action calculation)

The single non-trivial input to particle physics is the number 4 — the Euler characteristic of a six-dimensional geometric manifold. Every measurement in the Standard Model that does not involve \(m_e\) as a dimensional anchor traces back to this integer.
