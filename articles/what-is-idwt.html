What Is IDWT?

One wave. One integer. Two inputs. All fifteen particle masses, all forces, all quantum numbers — derived, not assumed.

One Wave on an Infinite-Dimensional Space

Infinite-Dimensional Wave Theory starts from a single postulate: reality is one complex wave Ψ∞ propagating on an infinite-dimensional manifold M∞. There is no separate electron field, no separate quark field, no separate photon field. There is one wave, and every particle is a bound resonance of it — the way a violin string has many harmonics but is still one string.

M∞ splits into two parts: ordinary time ℝt, and the sector manifold Ξ10. We live in the 3-dimensional observable universe, which in IDWT is Ψ∞ evaluated at a fixed position in the d>3 sector coordinates — we are inside M∞, not projecting down onto a screen from outside.

Ξ10 is not Planck-scale or compactified. It is macroscopic — its extent is set by the same coupling constants that determine particle masses. We are at d=3 of its ten directions; particles with wider wavefunction support vibrate across directions we never move through, and we measure only the d=3 component of their activity.

Six Sectors, Not Ten Random Dimensions

Ξ10 does not have 10 equal directions. It decomposes into exactly six sectors, each a geometrically distinct manifold, with dimensions d ∈ {2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10}. The sector set is not a choice — it is uniquely forced by two mathematical requirements: (1) the sectors must form a Hopf fibration chain, and (2) the coupling coefficient must reach a precise critical value. Those two conditions together admit exactly six sectors and no others.

The sectors are nested: every direction present in sector d is still present in all higher sectors. The d=2 directions are inside d=3, inside d=4, all the way to d=10. This is not a hierarchy of separate spaces — it is one space progressively revealing more of itself to particles with the capacity to use more dimensions.

The Mass Formula

Every particle has a mode index n and lives in one sector d. Its mass is:

m(n, d) = S(n, d) × mscale,d

S(n, d) = C(n+d−1, d)  =  (n+d−1)! / (n! (d−1)!)

S(n, d) is a binomial coefficient — it counts the number of ways to distribute n energy quanta across d sector directions. That is all it is. A particle with more sector directions available, or a higher mode number, can distribute its quanta in more ways, giving it a higher multiplicity and therefore more mass.

Example: The electron has n=13, d=6. So S(13,6) = C(18,6) = 18,564. The tau has n=23, d=10. S(23,10) = C(32,10) = 64,512,240. The d=10 sector shares the same mass scale as the d=6 charged-lepton sector, so the product 64,512,240 × mscale,10 × (1 + 1/1680) = 1776.84 MeV — the geometric back-reaction correction from the Gegenbauer critical point closes it to the PDG value of 1776.86 MeV.

The mass scales mscale,d are not free parameters — they are derived from one another by consistency conditions between sectors. The entire derivation runs from two inputs:

Input 1:   me = 0.511 MeV  (electron mass — the single dimensional anchor)

Input 2:   ns = 4  (the seed integer — an integer, not a continuous parameter)

Why ns=4? It is not a choice. It is the unique positive integer satisfying a fixed-point condition between sector d=4 and sector d=5. Two different cross-sector ratios are equal if and only if ns=4. No other value works.

From these two inputs, IDWT derives all six sector mass scales, all fifteen mode indices, and all fifteen Standard Model particle masses. The derivation is closed — no additional parameters enter.

Forces from Sector Geometry

Every force in IDWT is associated with a sector. Electromagnetism lives in d=2. The weak force also lives in d=2, as a different mode excitation of the same Hopf fiber. Color (the strong force) lives in d=4, in the CP² geometry whose isometry group is SU(3)/U(2).

Two principles together determine the full interaction structure of any particle.

Coordinate containment answers whether coupling is possible at all. A particle couples to a force only if the force's sector coordinates are inside the particle's sector. Since d=2 sits inside every higher sector (2 ⊂ 3 ⊂ 4 ⊂ 5 ⊂ 6 ⊂ 10), every particle has the photon's coordinates in its wavefunction — universal charge coupling. Since d=4 is not inside d=2 or d=3, photons and down quarks cannot couple to the strong force.

The coupling filter answers how coupling works and what is geometrically forbidden. The sector geometry of a particle is not just a label — it is a coupling structure. Polarization is not a property of the photon; it is what U(1) geometry of CP¹ produces as an electromagnetic coupling handle. Color is not a property of quarks; it is what the SU(3)/U(2) structure of CP² produces, giving exactly three coupling handles — χ(CP²) = 3. For neutrinos, the Clifford algebra of S⁵ at d mod 8 = 5 makes Majorana spinors impossible — not suppressed, structurally unavailable. Neutrinoless double beta decay has rate exactly zero. The electron's CP³ geometry cancels all color index contributions entirely, giving total QCD silence at every energy scale.

This generalizes polarization upward through the dimensions. The photon's two helicity states are what U(1) geometry produces at d=2. Moving to d=3, d=4, d=5, d=6 produces progressively more complex coupling structures — each one the natural analog of polarization in a higher-dimensional geometry. Quantum numbers are not inputs to the theory. They are what geometry does.

Gravity has no sector. It is curvature of the full M∞, sourced by mass through all sector coordinates equally. There are no gravitons. GN = G∞/V7, where V7 ≈ 113 is derived from the same sector coupling constants that determine particle masses.

What Comes Out

From ns=4 and me=0.511 MeV alone:

  • All 15 particle masses — electron, muon, tau, three neutrinos, six quarks, photon, W, Z, Higgs — reproduced to better than 1% without exception, most to better than 0.1%.
  • The number of quark colors Nc=3 is derived, not assumed. It equals the Euler characteristic χ(CP²) = 3.
  • All three PMNS mixing angles from the same sector coupling constants; the CP-violating phase δCP = π + 2θ13 ≈ 197.1° (PDG: ~197°).
  • Newton's constant GN = G∞/V7, where V7 ≈ 113 is the product of sector localization lengths, fully derived from the same coupling constants.
  • Zero neutrinoless double beta decay — an exact geometric result, not a dynamical suppression.
  • No gravitons, no supersymmetry, no Kaluza-Klein towers, no WIMP dark matter — each forbidden by a specific geometric theorem, not excluded by parameter tuning.

Why This Is Different

The Standard Model takes quantum numbers as inputs: quarks have three colors because experiment says so; the electron is colour-neutral because it has never shown strong coupling; neutrinos are probably Dirac because no Majorana mass has been measured. The values are fitted; the structure is axiomatized.

In IDWT, these are not axioms — they are theorems. Nc=3 is forced by the Euler characteristic of CP². Colour-neutrality for the electron is forced by index cancellation on CP³. The Dirac condition for neutrinos is forced by Clifford algebra at d mod 8 = 5. The sector quantum number of a particle is not a label attached to it — it is the geometry of its sector expressing itself as a coupling structure.

One wave. One integer. Two real inputs. Everything else follows.

The Geometry Is Here

There is a temptation to imagine the extra dimensions as somewhere else — deep inside matter at the Planck scale, or rolled up in some remote corner of space. In IDWT they are neither. M∞ is not a distant arena. It is here, at every point in the room you are sitting in, threading through every atom in your body.

The electrons in those atoms are not smeared-out clouds. They are orbiting in 6 dimensions — their wavefunctions are excitations of CP³, a six-dimensional geometric manifold. What the textbook calls the "electron cloud" is what an observer at d=3 resolves when they cannot access the three additional sector coordinates of that orbit: the d=3-coordinate component of a 6D trajectory. The s, p, d, f orbital shapes of chemistry are the d=3-coordinate structure of the modes, not the full structure.

The nucleus at the center of each atom is a d=3 object. The quarks inside it are forced by color confinement to cancel their d=4 character — the nucleus is a color-singlet, which in geometric terms means its d=4 footprint is zero. What remains is a d=3 structure: three-dimensional, fully at home in our observable space. That is why ordinary matter feels solid and three-dimensional. Our atoms are built around d=3 nuclei, and we are inside M∞ at the d=3 coordinate level.

But the electron orbiting that nucleus is not a d=3 object. It extends into three additional directions the nucleus does not occupy. From the electron's perspective, the nucleus is geometrically thin — present in only half of its coordinate space. The atom is not a point-charge wrapped in a cloud. It is a three-dimensional structure being orbited in six-dimensional space, bound together through the two shared dimensions of electromagnetism.

This is not a metaphor or an analogy. It is the literal geometry of M∞ operating at the scale of chemistry, in every molecule, in every cell, right now.
