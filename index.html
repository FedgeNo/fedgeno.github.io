The Core Idea

A single spinor field on an infinite-dimensional manifold. Everything else is derived.

  1. The fundamental object is a Dirac spinor field Ψ∞ on an infinite-dimensional manifold M∞. Particles are its resonant modes.
  2. Our observable 3D universe is the projection of Ψ∞ through the full space at a fixed hidden-sector address.
  3. The hidden dimensions are macroscopic, with particles bound by sector potential wells rather than compactified into Planck-scale geometry.
  4. Mass equals the cumulative count of hidden microstates accessible to a mode: m = m_scale,d × S(n,d).

M∞ has six stable sectors at dimensions d ∈ {2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10} — the levels at which the coordinate extension locks into self-consistent eigenstates. Each sector's particles couple to particles in other sectors because the sectors share coordinates: d=2 photon coordinates are literally inside every higher sector.

About the Theory

The Standard Model of particle physics is one of the most precisely tested theories in the history of science. It catalogs the electron, the quarks, the W and Z bosons, and every other particle we have found in accelerators, and its predictions match experiment to ten or more decimal places. Yet for all its accuracy, the Standard Model does not explain why the particles it describes have the masses they do. The electron is 0.511 MeV. The muon is 105.7 MeV. The top quark is 172,760 MeV. These numbers are inputs — measured, catalogued, and inserted by hand. No one knows why they are what they are.

Infinite-Dimensional Wave Theory asks: what is the simplest physical object that could, in principle, produce the full spectrum we observe? The answer it proposes is a single complex wave function Ψ∞ defined on a manifold of literally infinite dimension — not ten or eleven dimensions in the string-theory sense, but unboundedly many, with no fixed upper limit on the number of hidden directions. From this one object, every particle mass, every coupling constant, and every mixing angle in the Standard Model follows by calculation.

The setup rests on four postulates. Our observable universe is the projection of Ψ∞ through the full space at a fixed hidden-sector address — we access only modes that are resonant at our coordinate level. The hidden dimensions are macroscopic, with particles bound by sector potential wells. What we call mass is the count of hidden microstates associated with each mode — specifically, the number of ways to distribute n quanta across d hidden dimensions. This count is the combinatorial function S(n,d) = C(n+d-1, d), a simplex number. Gravity is standard 4D general relativity — the curvature of our 3+1D slice sourced by the IDWT matter fields through the effective stress-energy tensor. There are no gravitons and no Kaluza-Klein tower; G_N is a measured constant of spacetime.

The mass formula is then m = m_scale,d × S(n,d), where m_scale,d is a sector energy scale fixed by coupling self-consistency equations, and n is a mode index selecting which particle within a sector we are computing. Every particle in the Standard Model lives in one of six hidden sectors indexed by the dimension d ∈ {2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10}. These sectors are nested: the d=2 photon coordinates are literally inside every higher-sector wavefunction, and each sector adds directions that persist upward through every sector above it. This nesting is what determines which particles feel which forces. The only free inputs are the single integer n_s = 4 and the measured electron mass m_e. Everything else — all thirteen particle masses, the three gauge coupling strengths, the Weinberg angle, the Fermi constant, the CKM and PMNS mixing angles, and the complete neutrino mass spectrum — follows by calculation.

The accuracy of the predictions is what makes the framework interesting rather than merely speculative. The W and Z bosons come out within 0.003% and 0.047% of their measured values. The Higgs mass is within 0.013%. The muon lifetime is within 0.3%. All thirteen fermion and boson masses agree with PDG measurements to better than 0.8%, with most below 0.1%. The quark sectors have a uniform structural offset that is not a numerical accident but a consequence of the rank-1 approximation to the coupling kernel, which forces the same fractional offset to every mode in a sector.

The neutrino sector provides the most striking predictions. Neutrinos live in the d=5 hidden sector. The Clifford algebra of five-dimensional spinors has an exact mathematical property: when d mod 8 = 5, no Majorana condition can be imposed on the spinor. This means neutrinos must be Dirac fermions in IDWT — the geometry forbids Majorana mass terms and the seesaw mechanism entirely. As a consequence, the neutrinoless double beta decay rate is predicted to be exactly zero. No oscillation experiment was used to calibrate these predictions: the three neutrino masses, all three PMNS mixing angles, the mass ordering, and the beta-decay effective mass all follow from the same seeds n_s = 4 and m_e that fix everything else. The predicted sum Σm_ν = 60.39 meV satisfies the Planck bound and lies above the detection threshold of the CMB-S4 experiment.

IDWT is falsifiable in specific and testable ways. Any detection of neutrinoless double beta decay — by KamLAND-Zen, nEXO, or any successor experiment — would rule it out completely. A cosmological neutrino mass sum measured below 58 meV or above 62 meV would equally falsify it. An inverted neutrino mass hierarchy, if established, would be a direct contradiction. Within the framework, every number is a calculation rather than a parameter, and every calculation is reproducible from a publicly available Python script in under a second on a laptop.
