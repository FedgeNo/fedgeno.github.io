Drawing Orbitals from Orbits

In IDWT an electron is a single deterministic trajectory in six spatial dimensions, and a familiar atomic orbital is the 3D shadow of that path. This is a visual exploration of where those shapes come from — the angular lobes, the role of the viewing angle, and the concentric radial-node rings — built by rendering the orbits directly. It is a motivated classical illustration, not a first-principles derivation.

The setup

The electron lives on \(\mathbb{CP}^3\), a six-real-dimensional space, and a state is a degree-\(L\) monomial \(z_1^a z_2^b z_3^c z_4^d\). The trajectory is one continuous path: each complex amplitude \(z_k\) turns at its own frequency while the amplitude vector slowly precesses, because the moments of inertia of the six directions are not equal. We plot the three coordinates we would experience as space, \((\mathrm{Re}\,z_1,\ \mathrm{Im}\,z_1,\ \mathrm{Re}\,z_2)\), and render the result as a depth map: brightness is nearness to the viewer (bright = close, dark = far). No probability cloud and no ensemble — one particle, one path, seen from a chosen direction.

The angular shapes

The precession rate has one value that makes the projected shapes line up with the familiar shells. When the precession locks to the lead orbital frequency, \(\kappa = \omega_1 = \tfrac{3}{\sqrt2} \approx 2.1213\), a single monomial orbit projects to the right angular lobe count for every shell: a ring for \(s\), two lobes for \(p\), four for \(d\), six for \(f\) — that is \(2L\) lobes at degree \(L\). Away from that value the lobe counts come out wrong and the shape smears, so the value behaves like a resonance.

The number itself is suggestive: \(\tfrac{3}{\sqrt2} = \sqrt{9/2}\) can be written \(n_{\mathbb C}/\sqrt{d/n_{\mathbb C}}\) with \(n_{\mathbb C}=3\) (the complex dimension of \(\mathbb{CP}^3\)) and \(d=6\). We treat that as an observation, not a derivation — deriving the lock from the geometry is left open.

The same orbit, from every side

Because the picture is a depth map, what you recognize depends on where you stand. The clean orbital appears only when you look down the right axis; from other directions the very same trajectory reads as a three-dimensional body — a saddle, a barrel, a lantern. The shape is not in the orbit alone, it is in the orbit together with the viewing angle.

The radial rings

The lobes are the orbit's angular structure. The other half of the familiar picture — the concentric rings of a \(2s\), \(3s\), \(4s\) orbital, the "ring with a dot in the middle" — turns out to come from a second lock. On the spherically symmetric \(s\)-orbit, the radius is swept in and out by the orbit's breathing; when the depth oscillation is tuned to a whole-number multiple of that breathing, \(\omega_2 = N\,\omega_{\text{breath}}\), the depth map develops \(N-1\) concentric dark rings while staying circular. No extra ingredient is added — it is a second integer resonance among frequencies the orbit already has.

Why rings, exactly

The concentric rings are projection caustics — the fold lines where a curved sheet, seen edge-on, piles up. When the depth oscillation and the radial sweep are locked at an integer \(N\), the brightest-depth envelope as a function of screen radius is a Chebyshev polynomial \(T_N\), whose oscillations are exactly the rings. What this is, and isn't

This is an illustration of how much of the orbital picture a single deterministic 6D path can carry once you fix the precession and choose a viewing direction: the angular lobe counts, the strong dependence on viewing angle, and the concentric radial rings all appear from one construction, without a probability density. It is not a derivation. The precession value \(\kappa = 3/\sqrt2\) and the breathing frequency are chosen because they make the shapes come out, and several pieces are openly unfinished — why those frequencies, what selects \(N\) for a given orbital, and how to obtain the locks from the IDWT dynamics rather than imposing them. We keep the honest label.
