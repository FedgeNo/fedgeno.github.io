## 1. Candidate Resonances and the Co-fixed-point Condition

The framework treats every integer pair $(n,d)$ with $d \in \{2,3,4,5,6,10\}$ as a candidate resonance of $\Psi_\infty$ on $M_\infty$. The physical particles are those whose mode-sector pair $(n,d)$ is an element of $\mathbf{\Sigma_{\rm pairs}}$ — the unique finite closed set produced by the generation tower from the seeds $(1,3)$ and $(3,4)$ with offset-additive composite $(4,3)$ at depth 1. The generation tower assigns both the mode index and the sector; stability is a property of the pair, not of the mode index alone. A mode index $n$ appearing in a sector not assigned by the tower is not a physical particle.

This makes mode selection a geometric/combinatorial problem on $M_\infty$ itself: which $(n,d)$ pairs are stable resonances.

### 1.1 The Co-fixed-point Condition

A mode-sector pair $(n,d)$ is a physical particle if and only if it is an element of $\Sigma_{\rm pairs}$ — the unique finite closed set of 15 pairs produced by the generation tower (Part 1 §5, Part 2 §2–4). The tower is seeded by $(n_{\rm down}=1, d=3)$ and $(n_{\rm up}=3, d=4)$, with offset-additive composite $(n_s=4, d=3)$; it applies hockey-stick evaluations and additive operations that generate both the mode index and the sector assignment for every stable pair.
**Two independent derivation chains.** Membership in $\Sigma_{\rm pairs}$ requires both the correct mode index $n$ and the correct sector $d$. These come from separate sources: (i) the generation tower derives $n$ via hockey-stick, additive, and g-rule operations; (ii) the sector structure of Part 1 §3 assigns each particle family to its sector — quarks to $d=3$,4 via $\chi(\mathbb{CP}^2)=N_c$; neutrinos to $d=5$ via the Hopf fibration $S^1\to S^5\to\mathbb{CP}^2$; charged leptons to $d=6$ via $d{=}3\otimes d{=}3$ colour neutrality; tau to $d=10$ via Gegenbauer criticality; bosons to $d=2$ via the g-rule. Neither chain derives the other: $d$ cannot be recovered from $n$ alone, and the same HS operation applied to different seeds yields particles in different sectors. Concretely: $n=10$ is stable only as $(10,5)=\nu_1$, because the sector structure assigns the neutrino family to $d=5$ and the generation tower produces $n=10$ within that family. The pair $(10,6)$ is not a physical particle, not because of the tower but because no sector-structure rule places the $n=10$ particle in $d=6$.

**What the co-fixed-point condition excludes.** A mode index $n \in \Sigma_{\rm indices}$ appearing in a sector not assigned by the tower is excluded: the pair $(n,d)$ is not in $\Sigma_{\rm pairs}$. $\Sigma_{\rm pairs}$ is the complete co-fixed-point set; verified exhaustively up to $n \leq 1000$, no pair outside $\Sigma_{\rm pairs}$ is generated by the tower operations. The sectors $d=7,8,9$ appear in the coordinate hierarchy of $M_\infty$ but the current coupling construction does not establish active eigenmode geometry there (Rule A, §3a); they have no co-fixed-point tower and therefore no stable modes under the present framework.

### 1.2 Stability: Eigenvalue Selection by Coupling Closure 🔵

The free Hamiltonian $H_0 = \sum_d H_d$ has eigenvalues at $m_{\rm scale,d} \times S(n,d)$ for every integer $(n,d)$ — an infinite tower in each sector. The full Hamiltonian $H = H_0 + V_{\rm kernel}$ has a much smaller discrete spectrum. The stability of $\Sigma_{\rm pairs}$ follows from two independent mechanisms, proved at different levels of rigour.

**Mechanism 1 — l-parity protection (exact). ⭐**

The kernel $(ξ_d \cdot ξ_{d'})^2$ decomposes angularly into $l=0$ (scalar) and $l=2$ (tensor) components only. A single application of $V_{\rm kernel}$ can change the angular momentum quantum number $l$ of a sector mode by $0$ or $\pm 2$ at most. Starting from the seeds — which are at level $N=0$ ($l=0$) in $d=3$ — every mode reachable by any number of kernel applications must have **even** $l$.

Sector $d=3$, level $N=n-1$: the $l$-values present at level $N$ have the same parity as $N$. Level $N=3$ (the strange quark, $n=4$) contains only $l=1$ and $l=3$ — both odd. Level $N=1$ ($n=2$), $N=5$ ($n=6$), and all odd-$N$ levels contain only odd $l$.

Consequence: **the seeds at level $N=0$ ($l=0$) cannot reach any odd-level mode by any power of $V_{\rm kernel}$.** The odd-level non-$\Sigma_{\rm pairs}$ modes $n=2,6,8,10,\ldots$ are permanently disconnected from the vacuum. They cannot be excited from the seeds and cannot be sustained by the coupling chain. Their instability is exact and requires no self-energy calculation.

The strange quark ($n=4$, $d=3$, odd $l$) is similarly protected from intra-sector coupling to the seeds by $l$-parity, but is present in the spectrum as the offset-additive composite: $n_s = n_d + n_u = 1 + 3 = 4$, with composite level $N_{\rm comp} = N_1 + N_2 + 1 = 0 + 2 + 1 = 3$ (odd). The parity flip — even seeds producing an odd-$l$ composite — is the mechanism by which the offset-additive channel sources the odd-$l$ tree from the even-$l$ seeds; the offset quantum itself is the antisymmetric-pairing node derived in §1.2a. Its stability in the spectrum follows from cross-sector coupling $V_{34}$: the up quark ($n=3$, $d=4$) sits at level $N=2$ in the $d=4$ oscillator, which has an $l=0$ component, making the cross-sector coupling $(n=1,d=3) \to (n=3,d=4)$ allowed and providing the route back to the even-$l$ chain.

**Mechanism 2 — infinite-dimensional dephasing. 🔵**

Even-level non-$\Sigma_{\rm pairs}$ modes have nonzero off-diagonal matrix elements to other modes (ME($n=3 \to n=1$) $\approx 6.3$ sector units; `files/idwt.py` STEP 30). The $\Sigma_{\rm pairs}$ co-fixed-point modes are stable because the most dangerous (nearest-gap) same-sector channels are the odd-level neighbours, killed exactly by Mechanism 1 above; the remaining reachable even-$l$ neighbours are gap-dominated. The full-tower BdG check over all 15 modes confirms this: the worst-case ratio $|\Delta|/|\epsilon|$ is $0.148$ (attained by the seed $n=1$, $d=3$) and every reachable neighbour of every $\Sigma_{\rm pairs}$ mode has ratio $<1$, placing $\sigma(L_{n,d})$ on the real axis for all 15. Non-$\Sigma_{\rm pairs}$ even-level modes are not BdG-unstable (their gaps also exceed their couplings) — they are excluded by dephasing, not exponential growth. The amplitude of an even-level non-member disperses across the mode tower with no common revival period, because the cross-sector mass scales $m_{\rm scale,d}$ are incommensurate: every cross-sector ratio $m_{\rm scale,d}/m_{\rm scale,d'}$ is irrational except $m_{\rm scale,6}/m_{\rm scale,10} = 1$ (the μ–τ symmetric pair). The active sectors $d\le 10$ are a countable, discrete spectrum, however, so the survival amplitude is almost-periodic and recurs (Poincaré): the time-average is zero, but the pointwise amplitude returns, bounded at $\lesssim 4\times10^{-4}$. Exact non-return requires the reduced spectral measure to be continuous, which a discrete spectrum cannot supply — and the $d>10$ free dimensions cannot supply it either: the kernel has support only on sector coordinates $1..10$ (coordinate $j$ appears in the $(d,d')$ term iff $j\le\min(d,d')$), so translations of the coordinates beyond 10 are exact symmetries of the action, outer momentum is conserved, and an outer-uniform mode (Part 1 §3i) has exactly zero coupling width into that continuum at all orders (✅, `files/idwt.py` STEP 64). ### 1.2a The Offset-Additive Quantum as the Antisymmetric-Pairing Node 🔶

The offset-additive channel places every binary composite one mode index above the level-conserving combination of its constituents. Where two excitations at levels $N_a$ and $N_b$ combine, level conservation alone gives a composite at $N_a+N_b$ (mode index $n_a+n_b-1$), yet the physical composite sits at $N_a+N_b+1$ (index $n_a+n_b$). The strange quark is the depth-1 instance ($n_s=n_d+n_u=4$, $N_{\rm comp}=3$); the charged leptons repeat it ($e=\nu_1+n_u=13$, $\mu=\nu_2+n_c=35$, $\tau=\nu_3+n_d=23$). The extra quantum is the same object in every case, and it has a structural origin.

**The defect is a join count. ⭐** For any index relation $n_c=\sum_i s_i n_i$ with signs $s_i\in\{+1,-1\}$, the level defect $D\equiv N_c-\sum_i s_i N_i$ is, using $N=n-1$, independent of the operand values: $D=\sum_i s_i-1$ (`files/idwt.py` STEP 113). A binary composition ($++$) carries $D=+1$ whichever modes combine; an inclusion–exclusion combination ($++-$) carries $D=0$. The extra quantum is therefore a property of the act of joining two excitations into one, not of any particular pair. Equivalently, $S(n,d)=\binom{n+d-1}{d}$ counts multisets of $n-1$ quanta over $d+1$ directions — the $d$ sector directions and one condensate/ground direction whose unique empty multiset is the ground $S(1,d)=1$ — and the offset is one quantum seated on that ground per join.

**The join quantum is the antisymmetric-pairing node. 🔶** Because $\Psi_\infty$ is a single classical field, the configuration carrying two distinct excitations $a\neq b$ in their shared coordinates decomposes — by exchange symmetry alone, with no appeal to quantum statistics — into a part symmetric under $x_a\leftrightarrow x_b$ and a part antisymmetric under it. The symmetric part is even in the relative coordinate and maximal where the two excitations coincide; it is the two excitations *merged* into a single mode, at angular momentum $l=0$ and level $N_a+N_b$ — the level-conserving combination. The antisymmetric part is odd in the relative coordinate and vanishes identically at coincidence; it is the content that genuinely represents *two distinct* excitations. A composite that is a distinct pair is therefore the antisymmetric configuration, whose lowest excitation is one unit of relative angular momentum, $l=1$: a single node at coincidence. This node costs exactly one level in every sector — the cost of one angular quantum is dimension-independent, unlike the sector zero-point $(d-1)/2$ — reproducing the uniform $+1$ across the composites in $d=3$, $6$, and $10$ (`files/idwt.py` STEP 114). The cost and the symmetric/antisymmetric split are identities (⭐); the identification of a distinct composite with the antisymmetric channel is the structural input (🔶), consistent with the complement principle under which a newly selected resonance is the parity complement of the configuration the kernel itself produces.

**Ground-quantum forcing derives the antisymmetric channel for $n_s$ and $\tau$. ⭐** For composites in which one operand is the condensate ground $n_d=1$ (level $N=0$), no identification principle is needed. The symmetric merge of $(n_a, n_d)$ has index $n_a+n_d-1=n_a+1-1=n_a$ — the other operand, which already exists in the spectrum as a distinct mode. A composite that is the symmetric merge would therefore not be a new state at all; it is forced to the antisymmetric channel by elimination. This applies to $n_s=n_u+n_d=4$ (symmetric merge index $= n_u = 3$, existing seed) and to $\tau=\nu_3+n_d=23$ (symmetric merge index $= \nu_3 = 22$, existing neutrino) — both assignments are ⭐. The generation-1 and generation-2 charged-lepton edges $e=\nu_1+n_u$ and $\mu=\nu_2+n_c$ have both operands at non-zero level; their symmetric merge $n_{\nu_1}+n_u-1=12$ and $n_{\nu_2}+n_c-1=34$ are not existing members in general, so the identification remains 🔶 for those two edges (`files/idwt.py` STEP 114).

**The two charged-lepton edges are one ring operation, and the ring caps expel the third. ⭐** The deposit assignment (Part 2 §15) sites every mode at a bidegree $(\alpha,\beta)$ in the ring $R[\omega_2,\omega_3]/(\omega_2^3,\omega_3^4)$, where $\alpha$ counts $\omega_2$ ($\mathbb{CP}^2$, $d=4$) factors, $\beta$ counts $\omega_3$ ($\mathbb{CP}^3$, $d=6$) factors, and $\alpha+\beta+2$ is the sector dimension. In these coordinates the generational rule is a single operation rather than a per-generation choice of operand: each charged lepton is its same-generation neutrino plus one $\omega_2$ quantum, $\nu_1(0,3)\to e(1,3)$ and $\nu_2(1,2)\to\mu(2,2)$. The same-generation up-type partner sits in the same $\alpha$-column one $\omega_3$ below the neutrino — $n_u=\nu_1-\omega_3$, $n_c=\nu_2-\omega_3$, $n_{\rm top}=\nu_3-\omega_3$ — so "add the same-generation up-type quark" is exactly "add one $\omega_2$." The caps make this finite and forced: the $d=6$ sector (height $4$) admits exactly the two cells $(1,3)$ and $(2,2)$, matching the two-channel count at $d=6$, so it carries exactly two charged leptons; and the third neutrino $\nu_3(2,1)$ has no $\omega_2$-child, since $\nu_3+\omega_2=(3,1)$ violates $\omega_2^3=0$ while $\nu_3+\omega_3=(2,2)$ is the $\mu$ cell. The third charged lepton therefore cannot live in $d=6$ and is displaced off the tower to $d=10$ — a bidegree origin of the third-generation lepton anomaly that runs parallel to, and reinforces, the value-space statement that $\tau$'s tower operand $n_{\rm top}=72$ is an off-tower anchor (§1.3). What stays open is narrower than the operand rule itself. The two value increments — $\mathrm{val}(\alpha+1,\beta)=\mathrm{val}(\alpha,\beta)+\mathrm{val}(\alpha,\beta-1)$ at the two lepton cells, and nowhere else in the grid — are not the same kind of object. At $\mu$ the increment is Pascal: $\mathrm{val}(2,2)=\mathrm{val}(1,2)+\mathrm{val}(1,1)$ is $S(4,4)=S(3,4)+S(4,3)$, forced because $n_s-1=n_u$ makes the parent $S(n_s-1,4)=S(n_u,4)=n_{\nu_2}$, so $\mu$'s value is the lattice corner $S(4,4)$ and is closed (✅). At $e$ the increment is **not** Pascal: $13$ is not an $S(n,d)$ value at any sector dimension and $\{n_{\nu_1},n_u\}=\{10,3\}$ are not Pascal-adjacent, so $e=n_{\nu_1}+n_u=n_s^2-n_u$ attaches the up seed as a genuine additive node (the $\omega_2$ node, ⭐) and carries one irreducible input. The open charged-lepton core therefore reduces to this single generation-1 value $e=13$: $\mu$ is Pascal-closed, $\tau$ is ground-quantum forced, and $\nu_3$ is inclusion-exclusion forced, leaving 🔶 at $e$ alone (`files/idwt.py` STEP 115/116).

**This is the same fact as $l$-parity protection.** The relative $l=1$ node is odd, so it flips the level parity $P=(n-1)\bmod 2$. The kernel $(\xi\cdot\xi')^2$ is even ($l=0\oplus l=2$) and conserves $P$ exactly (Mechanism 1; `files/idwt.py` STEP 98), so it cannot produce the antisymmetric node from the symmetric combination it generates. One statement thus does double duty: it fixes the offset-additive quantum, and it shows the composite is unreachable from its constituents by the kernel — the offset-additive channel is a genuine selection input, not a coupling the kernel supplies.

**The third-generation neutrino takes no offset quantum.** The relation $\nu_3=\nu_1+\nu_2-n_u$ is an inclusion–exclusion, $D=0$: joining $\nu_1$ and $\nu_2$ would contribute one node, but $\nu_1$ and $\nu_2$ share the $d=4$ up-type substructure recorded by the $-n_u$ term — an already-joined overlap that removes exactly one node. ### 1.3 Quartic Bifurcation — The Bottom Quark

The bottom quark survives as a geometric-mean beat between two virtual $d=3$ modes rather than as a single simplex mode. The bifurcation point:

$$k_0 = n_{\rm strange}^2 = 4^2 = 16$$

1. $k_0 = n_s^2 = 16$ (seed self-product)
2. $k_0 = n_e + n_u = 13 + 3 = 16$ (cross-sector lepton + quark sum)
3. $k_0 = S(n_s,3) - S(2,3) = 20 - 4 = 16$ (intra-$d=3$ gap identity)

m_b &= \sqrt{S(16,3) \times S(17,3)} \times m_{\rm scale,3}\\
    &= \sqrt{816 \times 969} \times 4.702 \approx 4{,}181~\text{MeV}
\end{aligned}$$

That product character is why the bottom appears as a beat rather than a simplex mode and why the binomial restatement of the tower (Part 9 T0.5) reaches the top only through a $+1$ offset. Both are 3rd-generation quarks. Both are divisible by 8 — a consequence of $n_s = 4$ carrying the even factors ($16 = n_s^2$, $72 = N_c n_s N_f$ with $n_s N_f = 24$), an arithmetic observation about the factorizations and not a spinor selection rule, since no Clifford reality invariant singles out $n \equiv 0 \pmod 8$.

**The charged-lepton tower shows the parallel third-generation displacement ($\tau$: ⭐; $\mu$: ✅; $e$: 🔶).** Because $n_{\rm top} = 72$ is off the tower, it cannot serve as an operand either. Generations 1–2 fix the charged-lepton index by adding the same-generation up-type quark ($n_e = n_{\nu_1}+n_u$, $n_\mu = n_{\nu_2}+n_c$), but the third would be $n_\tau = n_{\nu_3}+n_{\rm top}=94$, so $\tau$ is instead displaced by one ground quantum to $n_\tau = n_{\nu_3}+n_d = 23$ (Part 2 §3). The $\tau$ displacement is ⭐ by ground-quantum forcing (§1.2a above): the symmetric merge of $\nu_3$ and $n_d$ would give index $\nu_3$, an existing member, so the composite is forced to the antisymmetric channel. The bottom beat and the $\tau$ displacement are two faces of one fact — a third-generation mode whose natural tower continuation is a product-form anchor ($k_0=16$ for the bottom, $n_{\rm top}=72$ for $\tau$) cannot follow the simplex tower and is reset by the ground quantum $n_d$. The neutrino value $n_{\nu_3} = S(3,5)+n_d = 22$ is a different case: it is not an index-addition (no two smaller tower indices sum to $22$) but the inclusion-exclusion mode $n_{\nu_1}+n_{\nu_2}-n_u$, equal to $S(3,5)+n_d$ only through $n_s=n_u+n_d$ (`idwt.py` STEP 112).

**Why the bottom displaces only in index but the $\tau$ also in dimension.** The two displacements are not quite symmetric, and the deposit grid says why. The twelve deposits (the $1,2,3,3,2,1$ hump, heights $0$–$5$, additive site $j=\alpha+\beta+2$) account for the photon and exactly eleven matter cells — every fermion except the bottom. **The bottom is the unique matter fermion off the deposit grid (⭐):** $d=3$ (height $1$) carries only two deposit cells (down, strange), and the third down-type is supplied not by a deposit but by the beat at $k_0=n_s^2=16$. Because the triple resonance defining that beat is a $d=3$ identity and $d=3$ is Gegenbauer-supercritical for $k_0=16$ ($b_{16}(3)>\tfrac12$), the beat binds in place: the bottom keeps its sector and displaces in index alone. The charged-lepton sector $d=6$ (height $4$) is the hump-symmetric partner of $d=3$, also with only two deposit cells ($e$, $\mu$); its third member is the corner deposit $\omega_2^2\wedge\omega_3^3$. The corner's additive site is $j=2+3+2=7$, but $d=7$ is inadmissible (the $7$–$9$ gap), and the corner is the top form on $\mathbb{C}^2\times\mathbb{C}^3=\mathbb{R}^{10}$, so it lands at the terminal admissible sector $d=10$: the $\tau$ displaces in dimension. The same $16$ fixes that destination — $4k_0=(d-2)^2$ selects $d=10$ as the unique sector where $k_0=16$ is Gegenbauer-critical ($b_{16}(10)=\tfrac12$). The asymmetry is therefore between an off-grid beat that binds where it forms and an on-grid corner whose natural dimension does not exist: index displacement for the bottom, dimensional displacement for the $\tau$ (🔵, resting on the Gegenbauer criticality of §3b and the $d{=}7$–$9$ inadmissibility; `files/idwt.py` STEP 117).
