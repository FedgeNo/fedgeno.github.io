Refractive Index from Sector Coupling

In IDWT, light does not slow in a medium. The photon is the d=2 mode on CP¹, which propagates null in M∞. Measured slowing is cross-sector phase matching.

Abstract

In Infinite Dimensional Wave Theory, light does not slow in a medium. The photon is the \(d=2\) mode on \(\mathbb{CP}^1\), which propagates null in \(M_\infty\). Measured slowing arises because the \(d=2\) photon field is delocalized over the local \(d=3\) and \(d=6\) matter background. The refractive index emerges from phase matching enforced by the cross-sector kernel. Effective phase slowness increases in proportion to the local mode densities \(\rho_3\) and \(\rho_6\) weighted by the combinatorial factors \(S(n,3)\) and \(S(n,6)\). We derive \(n(\omega)\) from the IDWT phase law and show how illustrative couplings reproduce \(n \approx 1.33\) for water and \(n \approx 1.52\) for BK7 glass at 589 nm.

1. Introduction

In the standard view, the refractive index \(n = c/v\) arises because light interacts with dipoles in matter. The macroscopic fields satisfy Maxwell equations with \(\varepsilon(\omega)\) and \(\mu(\omega)\), and the phase velocity is reduced to \(c/n\). Microscopically this is described as photon absorption and re-emission, or as a polariton quasiparticle.

In IDWT the picture is different. Time is not a dimension but the ordered parameter \(\mathbb{R}_t\). All dynamics occur on the infinite product manifold \(M_\infty = \mathbb{R}_t \times \Xi_\infty\). The photon is not a particle moving through \(d=3\) space. It is the \(d=2\) sector mode, supported on \(\mathbb{CP}^1\), which is already present everywhere the \(d=3\) spatial base exists. Propagation is null in \(M_\infty\).

What we call slowing is a projection effect. When the \(d=2\) mode traverses a region rich in \(d=3\) and \(d=6\) excitations, the cross-sector coupling forces phase matching. The local phase must accumulate extra cycles to stay coherent with the matter background. An observer who measures only arrival times in \(\mathbb{R}_t\) interprets this extra phase as a lower speed, hence \(n>1\).

2. IDWT Background

We adopt the core IDWT definitions:

  • Base manifold: \(M_\infty = \mathbb{R}_t \times \Xi_\infty\).
  • Active sectors: \(D = \{2,3,4,5,6,10\}\).
  • Mode count: \[ S(n,d) = \binom{n+d-1}{d} \]
  • Mass ladder: \[ m(n,d) = m_{\text{scale},d}\; S(n,d) \]
  • Phase accumulation law: \[ \frac{d\Phi_d}{d\omega} = \frac{2\pi}{m_{\text{scale},d}\; S(n(\omega), d-1)} \] where \(n(\omega)\) is the mode index resonant at \(\omega\).
  • Cross-sector coupling: \[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{int}} = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{d\neq d'} g_{dd'} \int (\xi_d \cdot \xi_{d'})^2\; [\bar{\Psi} P_d \Psi]\,[\bar{\Psi} P_{d'} \Psi]\; d\mu \] with projectors \(P_d\) and geometric overlap \((\xi_d \cdot \xi_{d'})^2 \ge 0\).

3. Photon Sector

The photon corresponds to \(d=2\), base \(\mathbb{CP}^1 \cong S^2\). Spinor dimension is 2, giving the two helicities. In \(M_\infty\) the mode is null, with no rest mass and no longitudinal degree of freedom. Its free phase slowness is

\[ \frac{d\Phi_2}{d\omega}\Big|_{\text{vac}} = \frac{2\pi}{m_{\text{scale},2}\; S(n(\omega),1)} \]

where \(S(n,1)=n\). In vacuum there is no \(d=3\) or \(d=6\) background to couple to, so this defines \(c\).

4. Matter Background

5. Derivation

We compute the refractive index as the ratio of effective to vacuum phase slowness for the \(d=2\) mode.

Step 1: local mode occupation

From mass density \(\rho_m\) and composition, estimate the number of active \(d=3\) and \(d=6\) modes per unit volume, \(\rho_3\) and \(\rho_6\). For neutral matter \(\rho_6 \approx Z/A \, \rho_3\).

Step 2: effective phase slowness

The cross-sector term adds a mean-field shift proportional to the overlap and local density. To first order:

\[ \frac{d\Phi_{2,\text{eff}}}{d\omega} = \frac{d\Phi_2}{d\omega}\left[ 1 + \frac{g_{23}\,\rho_3\, S(n,2)}{m_{\text{scale},2}} + \frac{g_{26}\,\rho_6\, S(n,5)}{m_{\text{scale},2}} \right] \]

where \(S(n,2)\) and \(S(n,5)\) appear because the coupling involves the projector densities on each sector.

Step 3: define refractive index

\[ n = \frac{d\Phi_{2,\text{eff}}/d\omega}{d\Phi_{2,\text{vac}}/d\omega} \]

Step 4: practical form

Absorb constants into effective polarizabilities \(\alpha_3\) and \(\alpha_6\):

\[ n(\omega) \approx 1 + \alpha_3\,\rho_3\, S(n(\omega),2) + \alpha_6\,\rho_6\, S(n(\omega),5) \]

with \(\alpha_3 = g_{23}/m_{\text{scale},2}\) and \(\alpha_6 = g_{26}/m_{\text{scale},2}\) in IDWT units. Frequency dependence enters through \(n(\omega)\) in \(S(n(\omega), d-1)\), via the resonance condition \(\hbar\omega \approx m_{\text{scale},d} S(n,d)\). Since \(S(n,2)=(n+1)n/2\) is quadratic and \(S(n,5)\) is quintic, \(n(\omega)\) increases with \(\omega\), giving normal dispersion.

7. Discussion

IDWT provides a geometric reason for \(n>1\). The coupling term contains \((\xi_d \cdot \xi_{d'})^2\), which is non-negative. Therefore the bracket in Step 2 is always greater than one, so \(d\Phi_{2,\text{eff}}/d\omega > d\Phi_2/d\omega\). No negative-index medium is possible from pure \(d=2\)-\(d=3\) coupling, consistent with natural materials.

The standard polariton picture says the photon mixes with matter excitations to form a slower quasiparticle. IDWT says the same mixing is already built into \(M_\infty\): the \(d=2\) mode is never separable from the \(d=3\) and \(d=6\) backgrounds where they overlap. What changes is not photon speed but the projected phase in \(\mathbb{R}_t\).

Dispersion follows directly from \(S(n,d)\). Higher \(\omega\) accesses higher \(n(\omega)\), which increases \(S(n,2)\) quadratically. This gives the observed increase of \(n\) toward the blue, without invoking separate oscillator strengths. Anomalous dispersion near absorption corresponds to a jump in \(n(\omega)\) when \(\omega\) crosses a matter sector resonance \(m_{\text{scale},3}S(n,3)\) or \(m_{\text{scale},6}S(n,6)\).

The model also explains why light appears already present across a medium in delayed-choice experiments. In IDWT the \(d=2\) field is delocalized over the whole \(d=3\) region; measurement selects a phase-consistent history, not a trajectory.

8. Conclusion

Refractive index is not a change in photon velocity. It is the measurable consequence of cross-sector phase matching in IDWT. The \(d=2\) photon accumulates extra phase in proportion to local \(d=3\) and \(d=6\) mode densities, weighted by \(S(n,2)\) and \(S(n,5)\). The simple linear form \(n(\omega) \approx 1 + \alpha_3\rho_3 S(n,2) + \alpha_6\rho_6 S(n,5)\) reproduces typical indices for water and glass and predicts normal dispersion from combinatorics alone. Fitting \(\alpha_3\) and \(\alpha_6\) to precision refractometry will determine \(g_{23}\) and \(g_{26}\), providing a direct optical measurement of IDWT sector couplings.
