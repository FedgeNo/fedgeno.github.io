Photon vs Electron in IDWT.

A Dimensional Comparison.

In Infinite Dimensional Wave Theory, the photon and the electron are not just different particles — they are resonances in fundamentally different hidden-sector geometries. This dimensional distinction elegantly explains many of their contrasting properties.

Detailed Interpretation

The Photon (d=2 sector)

  • Lives in the simplest non-trivial hidden sector: CP¹ (complex projective line, topologically S²).
  • The two polarization states are the complete internal geometry of this 2D space — there is nothing "hidden" beyond the two transverse directions.
  • n=0 mode with S(0,2) = 0 makes it exactly massless.
  • Because it is a pure gauge mode on the U(1) Hopf fiber, it projects extremely cleanly into 3D as a transverse wave. This is why light behaves so classically and propagates freely over long distances.

The Electron (d=6 sector)

  • Lives in a much richer 6-dimensional hidden sector (CP³ geometry).
  • Its wavefunction is a genuine standing-wave resonance in all 6 dimensions, confined by the sector potential.
  • What we observe in 3D is only a projection (at fixed hidden address ξ⁰) of this 6D object. The familiar s, p, d, f orbitals are low-dimensional shadows of the full 6D geometry.
  • The richness of 6D (higher multiplicity S(n,6), Kähler chirality for spin, etc.) produces the complex, probabilistic appearance of orbitals and the richness of chemistry.

Why This Distinction Matters

  1. Dimensional Hierarchy
    Photons are "simple" because they live in the lowest-dimensional sector. Electrons are "complicated" because they live in a higher-dimensional one. The electromagnetic interaction naturally couples them via the shared U(1) Hopf fiber.

  2. Projection Explains Quantum Weirdness
    The electron looks fuzzy and probabilistic in 3D precisely because we are seeing a 3D slice of a 6D deterministic wave. The Born rule emerges from the projection: |ψ_obs|² is the marginal probability density after "integrating out" the hidden coordinates.

  3. Polarization as Pure 2D Geometry
    The two polarization states of the photon are not an arbitrary choice — they are the full set of degrees of freedom of the d=2 sector. This gives a geometric origin for helicity and why photons have exactly two polarization states.

  4. Chemistry as 6D Projection
    Atomic orbitals, chemical bonding, and molecular shapes are the 3D shadows of 6D resonances. This explains why orbitals have nodes and lobes, why hybridization works, and why heavy elements show increasingly complex behavior (higher projection compression ratios).

This 2D-photon vs 6D-electron distinction is one of the most elegant and intuitive aspects of the IDWT framework. It turns two of the most fundamental objects in physics into natural consequences of their hidden-sector geometry.
