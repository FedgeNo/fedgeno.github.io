Orbitals as closed resonant orbits

In IDWT the electron is not a probability cloud — it is a single deterministic wave tracing one definite path. Drawn here is that path for the first orbital of each family — 1s, 2p, 3d, 4f — nested at a common nucleus. The familiar s, p, d, f lobe shapes are not put in by hand; they fall out of one closed trajectory in the electron's six-dimensional sector, \(\mathbb{CP}^3\).

What you are looking at

The electron lives in \(\mathbb{CP}^3\), a macroscopic 6‑real‑dimensional space — the \(d=6\) sector of \(M_\infty\). Its state at shell \(L\) is the degree‑\(L\) monomial \(z_1^a z_2^b z_3^c z_4^d\) with \(a+b+c+d=L\). The electron orbits, and the three coordinates we experience as space, \((\mathrm{Re}\,z_1,\ \mathrm{Im}\,z_1,\ \mathrm{Re}\,z_2)\), are plotted directly. Each colored curve is one electron's closed orbit; the bright point is the electron, the fading tail its recent path. No probability, no ensemble — one particle, one trajectory.

Why the lobes appear — a resonance

As the electron orbits, its amplitude slowly precesses while the phase sweeps around at the orbital frequency \(\omega_1\). When the precession locks to that orbital frequency — the resonance \(\kappa=\omega_1\) — the path closes into a rose, and the number of lobes is fixed entirely by the shell's degree:

LOBE COUNT
\[ N \;=\; 2L\,\frac{\kappa}{\omega_1}\,,\qquad \kappa=\omega_1\ \Rightarrow\ N=2L. \]

The monomial's moment‑of‑inertia gap is exactly \(L\), so the precession runs at \(L\,\omega_1\): the radius wobbles \(L\) times per turn, and taking the modulus doubles that — giving \(2L\) petals. The degree \(L\) enters twice, once as the inertia gap and once as the lobe count, bound together by the lock. (Verified numerically across the shells.)

The shells are nested at the real hydrogenic ratio. The smallest orbital of each type is the “circular” orbital \(l=n-1\) — 1s, 2p, 3d, 4f — whose most‑probable radius is exactly \(n^2 a_0\) with \(n=L+1\). That gives the nesting \(1:4:9:16\) you see, s tucked tight at the nucleus, f sweeping wide.

Why the shape is permanent

Every frequency in the orbit is commensurate — a rational multiple of one base — so the trajectory is a genuinely closed curve: it retraces itself forever rather than slowly filling the volume. Detune any one frequency and the lobes smear out into a featureless sphere. The closure is exactly what makes the orbital a stable shape instead of a transient.

What it means for IDWT

Textbook quantum mechanics hands you the orbital as \(|Y_L^m|^2\), a probability density over where a particle might be. Here the same lobe structure arises from geometry and determinism instead: a single classical path on \(\mathbb{CP}^3\), with the s/p/d/f counts produced by a frequency resonance tied to the monomial — that is, angular‑momentum — structure of the sector. It is a concrete picture of the central IDWT claim: what we read as quantum probability is the shadow of one deterministic wave, and the integers of chemistry (\(1,2,4,6\) lobes; \(1:4:9:16\) sizes) are consequences of where that wave lives.
