Atomic Orbitals as CP³ Projection

Every electron has a definite 3D location — the first three of its six coordinates. The s, p, d, f orbital families are the 3D shadow of the electron's full 6D orbit; Bohr levels, all multipole selection rules, Stark, Zeeman, and fine/hyperfine structure follow as theorems of CP³. At every shell with \(L \geq 1\), the SU(4) representation also carries states with angular momentum in the ξ-direction. These are not invisible particles: each is a real electron sitting at a definite 3D point with a real charge density. What Lemma 2 actually shows is narrower — they decouple from the standard orbitals, adding no new spectral lines.

Note: \(\Xi_6 = \mathbb{CP}^3\) is a macroscopic 6-real-dimensional space in IDWT, not a compactified extra dimension. The electron moves through all six dimensions of \(M_\infty = \mathbf{R}^3 \times \Xi_6\); our 3D space is the first three coordinates, and the electron always has a definite position there.

There are only two kinds of state at each shell, and both are genuine 6D orbits projected to their definite 3D position. The \(2L+1\) standard orbitals (blue) are the harmonic states — the ones textbooks call s, p, d, f. Their exact 3D shadow is the spherical harmonic \(Y_L^m\), so we trace that closed form and the familiar lobes accumulate. The additional states (pink) are every other state in \(\mathrm{Sym}^L(\mathbb{C}^4)\); each follows the deterministic torus orbit \(z_k(t)=\rho_k\,e^{i(\omega_k t+\phi_k)}\), and we draw its observable position — the first three of the six real coordinates, \((\mathrm{Re}\,z_1,\mathrm{Im}\,z_1,\mathrm{Re}\,z_2)\). Because they carry more of their motion in the \(z_4\)/\(z_3\) directions, their 3D shadow nests inward at smaller radius — but it is a real, definite charge cloud, never invisible. Everything is shown at once; the legend at upper left is just a key, and nothing can be hidden.

The Orbital Representations

At angular momentum \(L\), the orbital states form the holomorphic symmetric representation of \(\mathrm{SU}(4)\):

\[\mathrm{Sym}^L(\mathbb{C}^4), \quad \dim = \binom{L+3}{3}\]

Under the full subgroup chain, only the highest-spin \(\mathrm{SO}(3)\) irrep at each level projects to a standard orbital — the harmonic polynomials of degree \(L\) in \((z_1, z_2, z_3)\). These are the \(2L+1\) familiar spherical harmonics. Every other state in \(\mathrm{Sym}^L(\mathbb{C}^4)\) is an additional state: a real 6D orbit at a definite 3D location that simply isn't one of the textbook orbital shapes. That is the only distinction — see the Counting Theorem below.

Orbital Counting Theorem ⭐ Identity

Projection of \(\mathrm{Sym}^L(\mathbb{C}^4)\) to the observable \(\mathrm{SO}(3)\) selects exactly \(2L+1\) states — the harmonic polynomials of degree \(L\) in \((z_1,z_2,z_3)\). These are traceless symmetric tensors of rank \(L\), forming precisely the spin-\(L\) irrep of \(\mathrm{SO}(3)\); no lower-\(j\) irrep is harmonic at degree \(L\), which is why only the highest spin survives as the textbook orbital. The other \(\binom{L+3}{3}-(2L+1)\) are the additional states.

If you want the finer accounting, the additional states are of two algebraic types — but this is a basis label, not a difference in visibility: both are real 6D orbits sitting at definite 3D locations.

  • \(z_4\)-carrying — \(L(L+1)(L+2)/6\) states with at least one \(z_4\) factor (the ξ-direction). By ξ-orthogonality (Lemma 2) these decouple from the standard orbitals under any 3D operator, so they add no new spectral line — but each is still a real electron with a definite 3D charge density.
  • non-harmonic 3D — \(L(L-1)/2\) degree-\(L\) polynomials in \((z_1,z_2,z_3)\) with no \(z_4\). These are the traces; they carry the same \(\mathrm{SO}(3)\) content as orbitals already present at lower shells, so they introduce no new orbital shape.

The Exact Hamiltonian

Lemma 1 — Exact Potential Separability ✅ Structural

The total interaction potential on \(M_\infty = \mathbf{R}^3 \times \Xi_6\) is

\[V(\mathbf{r},\xi) = V_\mathrm{Coulomb}(|\mathbf{r}|) + V_6(|\xi|)\]

an exact sum — no \(\mathbf{r}\)-\(\xi\) cross terms. The proof has three parts: (i) the d=2 photon couples only to \(\rho_\mathrm{3D}(\mathbf{r})\), so the Coulomb term has no \(\xi\)-dependence; (ii) CP³ colour silence means the proton's quark modes create no \(\xi\)-dependent potential for the electron; (iii) the CP³ isometry makes \(V_6\) spherically symmetric in \(\xi\) and independent of \(\mathbf{r}\).

Because the potential separates exactly, the Schrödinger equation on \(M_\infty\) is exactly separable. The electron occupies sector mode \(n=13\), so \(\chi = \chi_{13}\) with \(E_\xi = m_e\). The 3D Hamiltonian is therefore exact — not an approximation:

\[H_\mathrm{eff} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e}\nabla^2_r - \frac{e^2}{|\mathbf{r}|} + m_e\]

Why ξ-States Don't Disturb the Standard Spectrum

Lemma 2 — ξ-Orthogonality ✅ Structural

Let \(O(\mathbf{r}, \nabla_r)\) be any 3D operator (no \(\xi\)-dependence). Let \(|\mathrm{obs}\rangle\) be any observable orbital state (angular part is a polynomial in \(z_1, z_2, z_3\) only). Let \(|\xi\rangle\) have nontrivial \(z_4\)-dependence. Then the off-diagonal element vanishes, \(\langle\xi|O|\mathrm{obs}\rangle = 0\) — the \(\xi\)-sphere integral of the cross term is zero by spherical harmonic orthogonality.

This is a statement about mixing, not presence. The diagonal expectation \(\langle\xi|O|\xi\rangle\) — including the charge density \(\rho(\mathbf{r})=\int|\Psi(\mathbf{r},\xi)|^2\,d\xi\) — is generally nonzero. A ξ-state is a real electron sitting at a definite 3D location; Lemma 2 says only that no 3D operator connects it to the standard orbitals.

The off-diagonal vanishing carries five results, one for each physical interaction class described by a 3D operator. In every case the consequence is that ξ-states neither add lines to nor perturb the textbook hydrogen spectrum — they decouple from it:

✅No new spectral lines (all multipoles) — Jμ(r) has no ξ-dependence; the ξ-to-orbital matrix element is zero at every EM vertex — E1, E2, M1, M2, … — so no radiative transition of any multipole order connects a ξ-state to an observable orbital. The standard line spectrum gains no extra lines.
✅Zeeman splitting — HB = −(e/2m)B·L is a 3D operator; it induces zero ξ–orbital admixture, so each shell gives exactly \(2L+1\) Zeeman energy levels (the \(m_L = -L,\ldots,+L\) substates) with the standard pattern intact.
✅Stark selection rules — the electric dipole operator er·E has no ξ-dependence; Lemma 2 kills all ξ–orbital mixing; standard \(\Delta L = \pm 1\) follows exactly.
✅Fine structure — HSO = (1/2m²c²)(1/r³)L·S is a 3D operator; no ξ–orbital admixture means no anomalous fine-structure shifts; the standard Dirac corrections are unmodified by the sector geometry.
✅Hyperfine structure — the hyperfine terms I·L, I·S, and δ³(r)I·S are all 3D operators; zero ξ–orbital admixture leaves the hyperfine spectrum exactly the standard result.
✅Definite 3D presence (what Lemma 2 does not say) — a ξ-state is not invisible. Its diagonal charge density \(\int|\Psi|^2\,d\xi\) is nonzero and contributes to the atom's total charge and electrostatic field, like any electron. What vanishes is only the off-diagonal coupling to the standard orbitals. "Decoupled from the orbital spectrum" is not "absent from 3D" — the particle is there, at a definite location.

Explicit Basis: L = 1

\(\mathrm{Sym}^1(\mathbb{C}^4)\) has basis \(\{z_1, z_2, z_3, z_4\}\).

Standard orbital (p): \(\{z_1, z_2, z_3\} \to (p_x, p_y, p_z)\). These are the standard \(Y_1^m\).

Additional state (1): \(\{z_4\}\) — a single basis vector along the SU(3)-singlet (\(z_4\)) coordinate. Its cross term with any monomial in \((z_1, z_2, z_3)\) under the SU(4)-invariant measure is zero (different monomials at the same degree are orthogonal). Hence \(\langle z_4 \,|\, O \,|\, z_i\rangle = 0\) for every 3D operator \(O\) and every \(i \in \{1,2,3\}\): it does not mix with the p-orbitals. It remains a real electron configuration with a definite 3D charge density of its own.

Structural prediction

ξ-states with \(z_4\)-direction angular momentum exist in every shell at \(L \geq 1\). Each is a real electron at a definite 3D location — it carries a charge density like any other — but Lemma 2 makes its off-diagonal coupling to the standard orbitals vanish for every 3D operator. So a ξ-state neither adds a line to the textbook hydrogen spectrum nor perturbs its fine, hyperfine, Zeeman, or Stark structure: it is decoupled, not invisible. The falsifiable content is the decoupling: if a transition were ever measured connecting a ξ-state to a standard orbital — a line that should not exist — the CP³ identification of the electron sector would be challenged.
