The Down Quark

The ground state of the observer's sector — the simplest particle there is, and the one whose mode index appears in every generation.

The simplest possible mode

Every particle in IDWT has two numbers: a sector dimension d and a mode index n. Its mass is S(n,d) × mscale,d, where S(n,d) counts the number of ways to distribute n quanta across d sector directions.

The down quark has n = 1, d = 3. The first thing to notice is that S(1, d) = 1 for every sector d, without exception. A mode at n = 1 always has exactly one microstate configuration — the single state where all quanta go to the same direction. There is no simpler excitation in any sector. The down quark is the most fundamental particle IDWT produces: it sits at the ground state of the sector that coincides with our observable 3D space.

mdown = S(1, 3) × mscale,3 = 1 × mscale,3 ≈ 4.702 MeV

S(1, d) = C(d, d) = 1   for every sector d

The mass of the down quark is simply the energy scale of the d=3 sector. Nothing multiplies it. n = 1 is not a mode that was chosen — it is the only mode that can exist before the spectrum begins. It is the base case of every hockey-stick sum in the theory.

Why n = 1 is forced

The two seeds that define the entire IDWT spectrum are ndown = 1 and nstrange = 4. The strange quark's seed requires an argument (it is the unique integer satisfying a fixed-point condition). The down quark's seed requires no argument at all — it is forced by the combinatorial structure of S.

S(1, d) = C(d, d) = 1 for any d ≥ 0. The ground state of any sector has exactly one microstate. Setting ndown = anything other than 1 would contradict the fact that S counts cumulative states from level 0 through n−1; a non-unit value at the ground state would imply a fractional or missing first level, which has no geometric meaning. ndown = 1 is not a choice or a fit — it is the unique consistent starting point.

This universality has a consequence: the down quark's mode index appears unchanged in every sector's generation law. When the hockey-stick identity closes the third generation, the closing term is always +ndown = +1. The down quark is the "+1" of the entire spectrum.

Why down is heavier than up

The down quark is heavier than the up quark — md ≈ 4.7 MeV versus mu ≈ 2.2 MeV — even though the down quark is the ground state (n = 1) and the up quark is at mode n = 3. A higher mode index normally means more microstates and more mass. So what is going on?

The answer is that the two quarks live in different sectors with different energy scales. The down quark (d=3) and the up quark (d=4) each have their own mscale, set by the sector coupling constants. The d=3 scale is larger than the d=4 scale by enough that even though S(3,4) = 15 amplifies the up quark's mass above the bare d=4 scale, the d=4 scale is small enough that the result stays below the d=3 ground state.

The ratio mu/md is not a free parameter. It is a theorem of the coupling structure:

mu/md = √(3/14)  ≈ 0.463     (Theorem S2)

This ratio equals √(3/14) because the coupling constants g33 = 8√7 and g44 = 12/√7, both derived from ns = 4, satisfy g33/g44 = 14/3 = (md/mu)². The squared mass ratio between the lightest particles of the two quark sectors is a geometric ratio of coupling constants — a ratio that comes from the seed integer alone.

The coupling constants also satisfy the exact product identity g33 × g44 = 96 (Theorem T9a). Both constants are computed from the same formula with ns = 4 — neither is independent of the other. The up-to-down mass ratio is therefore not a measured number inserted into the theory; it is the same number that fixes the PMNS neutrino mixing angles and the GTC correction to quark masses. Change one and every prediction in the theory shifts together.

The mass scale of d=3

The d=3 sector's energy scale mscale,3 is the down quark mass. It is derived from the coupling constant g33 through the self-consistency equations that link sector scales to one another. Specifically, the product g33 × g44 = 96 ties the d=3 and d=4 scales together, and once the electron mass me anchors the d=6 scale, every other scale follows through the chain of coupling ratios.

The IDWT value is mdown = 4.702 MeV, against the PDG central value of 4.67 MeV — an error of +0.68%. This offset is structural: the rank-1 approximation to the coupling kernel forces exactly the same fractional offset onto every regular mode in a sector. The down and strange quarks both show +0.68% because both are regular modes of d=3, subject to the same correction. This offset is not a free parameter tuned to one particle — it is the same number across all regular modes in the sector, and it arises from the difference between the rank-1 kernel and the full kernel, not from a missing constant.

The down quark's sector

The d=3 sector has geometry S³ — the three-sphere — with isometry group SO(4). Unlike every other sector except d=5, it is a real manifold rather than a complex projective space. This absence of complex (Kähler) structure means the d=3 sector is vector-like: its left and right handedness are not separated by the sector geometry itself. SO(4) = SU(2)L × SU(2)R gives the left-handed weak isospin coupling, with the right-handed SU(2)R present in the geometry but not generating an observed coupling.

The d=3 sector is also our observable space. The coordinate nesting Ξ2 ⊂ Ξ3 ⊂ Ξ4 ⊂ ... places the d=3 sector at the same dimensional depth we occupy. Down quarks are not hidden in extra dimensions — they vibrate in the same three spatial directions we move through. This is why the quark content of a proton contributes to its inertia and gravity in a geometrically straightforward way: d=3 wavefunctions and the d=3 coordinates we resolve are the same coordinates, giving a unit overlap rather than the fractional readout we get for higher-d modes.

The colour charge of the down quark is inherited, not intrinsic. It comes from the coordinate containment relation d=3 ⊂ d=4: the down quark's wavefunction has support in the d=4 CP² directions, where colour originates. The d=3 sector's own coupling contribution is the weak isospin structure; the colour coupling is what CP² geometry produces when the d=3 wavefunction extends into d=4.

ndown = 1 closes the third generation

The three generation laws all follow from the hockey-stick identity S(n,d) = S(n,d−1) + S(n−1,d) applied at specific (n,d) pairs. The third generation law is:

nτ = nν₃ + ndown = 22 + 1 = 23

The tau lepton's mode index is the third neutrino's mode plus exactly the down quark's mode index. Since ndown = S(1, d) = 1 for every d, the "+1" in this identity is not specific to d=3 — it is the universal ground state appearing at the closing step of the generation tower. The down quark contributes the base case. Without ndown = 1, the generation tower does not close.

This is also why the tau is the heaviest lepton. The third-generation closing adds the minimum possible increment — the ground-state unit — to the largest neutrino mode index. Any other choice of closing term would either produce a tau that is lighter than it is or would require a mode index not in the observed spectrum. The structure is uniquely determined.
